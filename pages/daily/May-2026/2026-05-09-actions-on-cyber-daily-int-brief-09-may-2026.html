Executive Summary

The most urgent issue for UK small businesses, charities, clubs and community groups today is a new wave of high-risk edge and admin portal vulnerabilities being actively tracked by CISA and vendors. Of the listed KEV items, Palo Alto PAN-OS CVE-2026-0300 has the tightest deadline today (09 May 2026), and CISA has also added Ivanti EPMM, LiteLLM, Linux kernel and cPanel-related issues to its exploited list. For non-technical organisations, the practical message is simple: if you use any of these products, confirm patch status now, apply vendor mitigation guidance, and ask your provider whether you are exposed. Situation

CISA’s Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog is the clearest signal of what attackers are actively using in the wild, and it should be treated as a priority list rather than a general vulnerability feed. On the UK side, the NCSC has recently warned about attackers abusing vulnerable edge devices and public-facing services, which makes internet-facing infrastructure and admin portals especially important to check. For smaller organisations, this is usually a supplier or hosted-service issue rather than an in-house technical project, so the first step is to identify whether you use any affected products or rely on a managed provider that does. ([cisa.gov](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog?f%5B0%5D=vendor_project%3A800&f%5B10%5D=vendor_project%3A842&f%5B11%5D=vendor_project%3A845&f%5B12%5D=vendor_project%3A851&f%5B13%5D=vendor_project%3A854&f%5B14%5D=vendor_project%3A858&f%5B15%5D=vendor_project%3A866&f%5B16%5D=vendor_project%3A872&f%5B17%5D=vendor_project%3A886&f%5B18%5D=vendor_project%3A892&f%5B19%5D=vendor_project%3A893&f%5B1%5D=vendor_project%3A801&f%5B20%5D=vendor_project%3A900&f%5B21%5D=vendor_project%3A902&f%5B22%5D=vendor_project%3A909&f%5B23%5D=vendor_project%3A915&f%5B24%5D=vendor_project%3A917&f%5B25%5D=vendor_project%3A927&f%5B26%5D=vendor_project%3A931&f%5B27%5D=vendor_project%3A938&f%5B28%5D=vendor_project%3A940&f%5B29%5D=vendor_project%3A945&f%5B2%5D=vendor_project%3A809&f%5B3%5D=vendor_project%3A813&f%5B4%5D=vendor_project%3A817&f%5B5%5D=vendor_project%3A820&f%5B6%5D=vendor_project%3A823&f%5B7%5D=vendor_project%3A824&f%5B8%5D=vendor_project%3A828&f%5B9%5D=vendor_project%3A837&page=1&utm_source=openai))

Who should care

  • Charities and community groups using hosted web platforms, admin portals, or outsourced IT
  • Small businesses running internet-facing security appliances or remote access services
  • Organisations using cPanel/WHM or WordPress hosting platforms
  • Teams using Ivanti EPMM for mobile device management
  • Any organisation that relies on a third-party IT provider to patch and monitor systems

Why it matters

A public-facing vulnerability in a firewall, VPN, hosting panel or mobile management platform can be used as an entry point into the rest of your systems. For small organisations, that can mean account takeover, website defacement, email compromise, data theft, or ransomware disruption. The practical risk is not only the flaw itself, but slow patching, forgotten hosted services, and providers that have not yet confirmed whether they are affected. ([security.paloaltonetworks.com](https://security.paloaltonetworks.com/CVE-2026-0300?utm_source=openai))

Actions On

  1. Ask your IT provider or hosting company today whether you use PAN-OS, Ivanti EPMM, LiteLLM, cPanel/WHM, WP2, or affected Linux kernel versions.
  2. If you use Palo Alto PAN-OS, confirm whether CVE-2026-0300 is patched and whether User-ID Authentication Portal access is restricted to trusted zones or disabled if unnecessary.
  3. If you use Ivanti EPMM, LiteLLM, or cPanel/WHM/WP2, request confirmation of patching and any compensating controls already in place.
  4. Check whether any internet-facing admin portal, VPN, firewall, mobile device manager, or hosting panel is exposed to the public internet.
  5. Review recent sign-in alerts, account changes, and website or mail logs for unusual activity.
  6. Make sure backups are current and that you know how to restore key services if a provider asks you to take a system offline.

Question to ask your IT provider

Are we using any of the five CISA KEV items listed today, are they patched, and if not, what compensating controls or service changes are in place right now?

After-action review

  • Did we identify every internet-facing system and provider service that matters to us?
  • Did our provider confirm patching or mitigations for any KEV-listed products?
  • Do we have a named owner for urgent vendor advisories in future?
  • Would we know who to call and what to disconnect if an exposed service were compromised?
