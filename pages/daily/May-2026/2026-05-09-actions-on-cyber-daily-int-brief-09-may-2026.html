Relevance rating: Act Now

Executive Summary

The most urgent issue for UK small organisations today is a critical Palo Alto Networks PAN-OS vulnerability in the User-ID Authentication Portal. Palo Alto says the issue is being actively attacked, and the workaround is to restrict portal access to trusted zones or disable it if it is not needed. For any organisation that uses Palo Alto firewalls, this should be treated as an immediate check-and-fix item. If you do not use Palo Alto, the broader message still matters: edge devices and remote access systems are high-value targets, and exposed admin or authentication services should be reviewed now. ([security.paloaltonetworks.com](https://security.paloaltonetworks.com/CVE-2026-0300?utm_source=openai))

Situation

Palo Alto Networks published an advisory for CVE-2026-0300 on 5 May 2026. The company says the flaw affects PAN-OS User-ID Authentication Portal and is being attacked in the wild. The vendor notes that risk is reduced when access to the portal is limited to trusted internal IP addresses, and that Prisma Access, Cloud NGFW and Panorama are not impacted. For non-technical leaders, the practical point is simple: if you have a Palo Alto firewall in use, ask whether the User-ID Authentication Portal is enabled and whether it is exposed beyond trusted zones. ([security.paloaltonetworks.com](https://security.paloaltonetworks.com/CVE-2026-0300?utm_source=openai))

Who should care

  • Any organisation using Palo Alto Networks PAN-OS firewalls
  • IT providers and managed service providers supporting firewalls or remote access systems
  • Small businesses, charities and clubs that rely on externally exposed admin portals
  • Organisations without in-house security teams that depend on a supplier for patching and perimeter protection

Why it matters

Edge devices are a common way into small organisations because they sit on the internet and often support remote access, login or admin functions. When a flaw is already being attacked, the priority is not investigation first, but reducing exposure first. If your supplier says the service is not needed, switch it off. If it is needed, confirm it is restricted to trusted zones and patched or mitigated as soon as possible. This is especially important for small organisations that may not notice compromise quickly. ([security.paloaltonetworks.com](https://security.paloaltonetworks.com/CVE-2026-0300?utm_source=openai))

Actions On

  1. Check whether your organisation uses Palo Alto PAN-OS and whether the User-ID Authentication Portal is enabled.
  2. If the portal is not required, disable it now.
  3. If the portal is required, restrict access to trusted internal zones only, as the vendor advises.
  4. Ask your IT provider whether PAN-OS devices are on a fixed, vulnerable version and whether the vendor fix has been applied.
  5. Review all internet-facing admin, login and remote-access services for anything that can be turned off or narrowed to trusted addresses.
  6. For the KEV items, confirm whether you use any of the listed products and put each one on a same-day or next-business-day remediation plan based on the due date.

Question to ask your IT provider

Do we use any Palo Alto PAN-OS devices, is the User-ID Authentication Portal enabled, is it restricted to trusted zones only, and have you applied the vendor mitigation or upgrade for CVE-2026-0300? Please also confirm whether any of the other CISA KEV items listed today affect us.

After-action review

  • Did we identify every exposed firewall, VPN, admin portal and remote-access service we run?
  • Did we confirm whether the affected feature was enabled and externally reachable?
  • Did we record who owns the fix and the deadline for each KEV item that applies to us?
  • Did we ask the supplier for written confirmation of patching or mitigation?
  • Did we check for signs of unusual access or alerts around the affected system?
