ServiceNow customer data exposure: expect targeted phishing and supplier‑themed scams

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 10 June 2026, 12:03 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier incident scams and targeted phishing off the back of a SaaS data exposure

What to look out for today

Supplier-themed phishing and fraud attempts using “ServiceNow security incident” wording to trick staff into handing over logins, approving MFA prompts, downloading “incident reports”, or changing payment details.

Also watch for fake “urgent Microsoft update” emails and opportunistic login attempts as attackers react to widely publicised monthly fixes.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Service desk and workflow tools sit in the middle of everything: tickets can include customer details, internal contacts, supplier info, invoices, change requests, and IT reset workflows.
  • Ripple effects: even if your organisation wasn’t directly affected, criminals may use the news as a believable pretext to contact your finance team, helpdesk, or managers.
  • More convincing targeting: exposed contact details (names, emails, roles) can make spear-phishing look legitimate and increase the chance of successful Business Email Compromise (BEC) or payment diversion.

Warning signs

  • Emails or calls claiming to be from a supplier/IT provider asking you to “verify your ServiceNow/Microsoft account” or reset passwords immediately.
  • Unexpected requests to approve an MFA prompt, especially after an email that says “security verification required”.
  • Messages containing links to “incident portals”, “support cases”, or “secure documents” you weren’t expecting.
  • Invoices or bank detail changes justified by “security incident”, “system migration”, or “new payment process”.
  • Unusual password reset emails, new device login alerts, or ticket activity outside normal hours.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Pretext phishing: using the supplier incident as cover to harvest Microsoft 365/Google Workspace credentials or to capture MFA codes.
  • Helpdesk manipulation: calling your IT support pretending to be a staff member “locked out after the incident” to trigger an account reset.
  • Payment diversion: targeting finance teams with “updated bank details due to incident response” and pushing urgency.
  • Data-driven targeting: if attacker-obtained details include names/roles, they can craft believable messages aimed at approvers and administrators.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (10 minutes): “No one should click ServiceNow/Microsoft ‘security incident’ links from email. Use bookmarks or your known login address.”
  • Lock down payment changes: require a callback to a known number (from your records, not the email) for any bank detail change.
  • Check your logs/alerts: look for unusual sign-ins, repeated MFA prompts, or spikes in password resets for key users (admins, finance, payroll).
  • Review who has admin rights in key SaaS tools and remove any that aren’t essential.
  • If you use ServiceNow: confirm with your account owner/IT provider what data may have been exposed and what notifications you should expect.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we use ServiceNow directly or via a supplier/MSP? If yes, which instances and what data types are stored in it (tickets, attachments, customer data, credentials, API keys)?
  • Have we received (or should we expect) any supplier notifications? Who is monitoring those inboxes?
  • What extra monitoring is in place this week for unusual sign-ins, MFA fatigue attacks, and mailbox forwarding rules?
  • Are our break-glass/admin accounts protected with strong MFA and isolated from day-to-day email use?
  • What is our process if finance receives a “security incident” payment-change request—can you help us enforce it?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Microsoft’s June update cycle is unusually large and includes fixes for issues that were being actively discussed, which can drive a short-term spike in scanning, phishing and “fake update” lures. For SMEs, the key is operational: ensure your IT provider has a plan and timeline to roll out updates safely (including laptops off-site), and remind staff that updates should only come through normal device prompts—not via emailed links.

One action today

Send a short internal note today: “No ServiceNow/Microsoft ‘incident’ links from email—use bookmarks; and all bank detail changes must be verified by callback to a known number.”
