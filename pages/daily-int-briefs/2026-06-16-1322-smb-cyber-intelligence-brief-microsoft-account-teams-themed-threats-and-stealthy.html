SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: Microsoft account/Teams-themed threats and stealthy cloud email rule abuse

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Tuesday 16 June 2026, 13:22 UK time
Today’s look-out: Microsoft 365/Teams impersonation and cloud email rule abuse (plus security appliance exploitation)

What to look out for today

Today’s key theme is attackers hiding in plain sight by impersonating Microsoft security alerts and blending malicious activity into everyday cloud services (Microsoft Teams, Google Workspace). Separately, there are reports of active exploitation against a Fortinet security platform used by some organisations and managed service providers.

  • Fake Microsoft account security alerts used in spear-phishing to trick recipients into opening or following malicious content.
  • Ransomware activity that “hides” traffic via Microsoft Teams infrastructure, making it harder for basic network controls to spot.
  • Cloud email rule tampering (e.g., Google Workspace rules) to quietly copy or forward sensitive emails without obvious logins.
  • Data breach ripple effects: high-profile breach claims can trigger convincing follow-on scams (fake “password reset”, “secure portal”, or “urgent compliance” messages).

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Most SMEs rely on Microsoft 365, Teams, and cloud email to run finance, sales and day-to-day operations. Attackers don’t need to “take everything down” to cause real damage: silently redirecting invoices, capturing email threads, or taking over admin accounts is often enough to enable fraud or ransomware.

Even if you’re not a direct target, supplier and sector incidents can be used as believable hooks to trick your staff into handing over credentials or approving payment changes.

Warning signs

  • Emails claiming “Microsoft security alert”, “unusual sign-in”, or “account will be locked” that push urgency.
  • Requests to sign in to view a message, secure a document, or verify your account when you weren’t expecting it.
  • In Teams: unexpected chats, new “external” contacts, or links/files from people you don’t normally work with.
  • Users reporting missing emails, strange auto-replies, or colleagues saying they received odd messages “from you”.
  • Finance team sees bank detail changes or new payee requests arriving mid-thread, especially if phrased as confidential/urgent.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Phishing for Microsoft 365 credentials, then using the mailbox to reset other passwords and impersonate staff for payment fraud.
  • Creating or modifying mail rules (e.g., forwarding/copying certain subjects, hiding replies, or routing invoices) to maintain stealth.
  • Blending command-and-control traffic into trusted cloud infrastructure (e.g., Teams-related traffic), which can delay detection.
  • Leveraging breach news (e.g., claims involving major institutions) to craft believable “action required” messages to your staff or customers.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): Microsoft “security alert” emails and unexpected Teams messages are high-risk. Use the normal sign-in route (bookmark) rather than email links.
  • Lock down payment changes: require a callback to a known number (from your records, not the email) for any bank detail change.
  • Check for suspicious inbox rules/forwarding: ask IT to review for unusual forwarding, hidden rules, or new delegated access on key mailboxes (finance, payroll, directors).
  • Ensure MFA is on for all users and that admin accounts are separated from day-to-day email use.
  • Prepare for disruption: confirm you can still operate if email/Teams is temporarily disabled during an incident (e.g., out-of-band contact list).

Ask your IT provider

  • Are we monitoring for new or modified mailbox rules/forwarding in Microsoft 365 and/or Google Workspace, especially on finance and leadership accounts?
  • Do we have alerts for unusual sign-ins and impossible travel on key accounts, and who responds after hours?
  • What controls do we have for Teams external access and suspicious links/files shared in chats?
  • If ransomware activity hides in trusted cloud traffic, what endpoint protection and logging do we rely on to detect it?
  • Do we operate or manage any Fortinet FortiSandbox for the business (or for any shared service), and what is the current status of mitigations/updates and monitoring?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

There are reports of active exploitation involving Fortinet’s FortiSandbox platform. If you use it directly or via an MSP, treat this as time-sensitive: confirm whether you have it, whether it is internet-facing, and whether your provider has applied vendor-recommended updates/mitigations and increased monitoring.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “Do not click Microsoft ‘security alert’ links or unexpected Teams files; sign in via your saved bookmark, and report anything urgent/odd to IT immediately.”
