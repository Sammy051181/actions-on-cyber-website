SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: software supply‑chain risk, smishing surge, and fake breach notices

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Saturday 13 June 2026, 11:07 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier & software supply‑chain compromise + smishing/phishing themes

What to look out for today

  • Software supply-chain compromise: attackers hijacked a large number of community-maintained Linux packages (Arch Linux AUR) by altering build scripts to install credential-stealing malware when built.
  • Smishing (phishing by text message): reports of organised smishing operations using AI tooling to help generate and run convincing phishing campaigns at scale.
  • “Breach news” may be weaponised: a US state temporarily took its breach notification portal offline after fake breach disclosures appeared—expect scammers to reference “public breach reports” to create urgency.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs often rely on outsourced IT, web developers, and third-party software. Supply-chain issues can hit you indirectly (e.g. your website provider, MSP, or a contractor’s laptop) and lead to account takeover, invoice fraud, or wider malware spread. At the same time, smishing remains a high-conversion route into Microsoft 365, banking, payroll, and other business systems.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected password reset or MFA prompts for Microsoft 365/Google/work accounts.
  • Staff receiving delivery fee, tax refund, missed parcel, or “account locked” text messages that push them to click a link.
  • New or unusual admin accounts, email forwarding rules, or changes to supplier bank details.
  • Developers/IT noticing unusual package behaviour during installs/builds, or tools suddenly requesting broader permissions than expected.
  • Messages claiming “you’ve been listed in a breach portal / public notice” and urging immediate payment or urgent verification.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Supply-chain route: compromise developer or IT endpoints via tampered packages, then steal credentials/tokens (e.g. email, cloud admin, source code, remote access tools), enabling follow-on compromise of client environments.
  • Smishing route: send high-volume, realistic texts (potentially AI-generated) leading to fake login pages to capture passwords and MFA codes, or to persuade staff to approve a sign-in.
  • Social proof route: reference “public breach disclosures” (even if fake) to pressure finance or HR into acting quickly—often as a pretext for payment diversion or credential capture.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (10 minutes): remind everyone that texts about deliveries, banking, payroll, or “account security” should be treated as suspicious—use known bookmarks/apps rather than tapping links.
  • Tighten payment-change controls: re-validate any supplier bank detail change using an out-of-band method (call a known number from your records, not the email/text).
  • Check admin hygiene: review Microsoft 365/Google admin accounts, MFA status, and any new forwarding/inbox rules.
  • Contractor/MSP check-in: if you use Linux-based build systems or developers, ask what they’re doing to reduce risk from third-party/community packages and how they monitor for credential theft.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have conditional access / sign-in risk alerts enabled for our email accounts, and who responds to them out of hours?
  • How do we detect and respond to email rule changes (auto-forwarding, hidden inbox rules) and new admin creation?
  • For developers/website suppliers: what controls are in place for software supply-chain risk (approved sources, review of third-party packages, secrets management, monitoring for token/credential theft)?
  • If a staff member clicks a smishing link, what is our first-hour playbook (password reset, session revoke, MFA review, bank notification, device checks)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your organisation runs an old or lightly maintained website/forum, note that phpBB has fixed a long-standing authentication bypass issue. For SMEs, the practical takeaway is to identify any customer-facing forums or legacy web apps you still host (including on cheap shared hosting) and make sure someone is responsible for updates, admin account review, and backups.

One action today

Send a same-day internal note: “Do not click links in text messages—use bookmarks/apps instead; and all supplier bank-detail changes must be verified by calling a known number.”
