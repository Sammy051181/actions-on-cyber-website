Today’s SMB cyber lookout: macOS info‑stealers and account‑takeover fraud

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Thursday 25 June 2026, 19:24 UK time
Today’s look-out: Mac malware + account takeover and fraud prevention hygiene

What to look out for today

1) macOS malware targeting data theft: Reporting highlights a newly identified macOS implant/info-stealer (“Gaslight”) designed to steal information and to confuse AI-assisted analysis tools used by defenders.

2) Account takeover and fraud patterns: A separate story on a DraftKings account-hacking case is a timely reminder that criminals still monetise credential theft and reused passwords through large-scale account takeover.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • More Macs in business: Many SMEs have Macs in leadership, finance, design, and sales. Info-stealers can lead to email compromise, cloud account takeover, and invoice/payment fraud.
  • Knock-on risk: If an attacker gets into a mailbox or password manager, they can pivot into Microsoft 365/Google Workspace, payroll, accounting, CRM and file storage.
  • Detection gets harder: Malware that tries to mislead automated/AI tooling increases the chance that a small IT team or outsourced provider misses warning signals.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected login prompts, security alerts, or MFA push requests users didn’t initiate.
  • New “device signed in” notifications for email, cloud storage, or business apps.
  • Rules/forwards in mailboxes you didn’t set (e.g., auto-forward to external addresses).
  • Customer/supplier messages claiming “bank details have changed” or urging urgent payment changes.
  • Unexplained browser pop-ups, new extensions, or repeated “error” messages that coincide with odd account activity.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Steal credentials/session tokens from endpoints, then log in to cloud services as the user.
  • Abuse email access to watch invoice conversations and then insert a fraudulent payment request at the right moment.
  • Leverage password reuse across services to take over accounts at scale (common in consumer platforms, but the same approach works against business SaaS).
  • Reduce defender confidence by seeding misleading strings/data intended to trip up AI-assisted triage and analysis.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff note: “No payment detail changes without a call-back to a known number; report unexpected MFA prompts immediately.”
  • Check your essentials: Ensure MFA is enabled on email, accounting, payroll and file storage, and that it’s enforced for all users (including admins).
  • Review mailbox forwarding and rules for finance and senior staff accounts.
  • Prioritise Mac coverage: Confirm your endpoint protection is installed, up to date, and reporting for macOS devices (not just Windows).
  • Strengthen passwords: Require unique passwords (ideally via a password manager) to reduce credential-stuffing/account takeover risk.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have the same level of monitoring and alerting on macOS as we do on Windows?
  • How quickly would we know if an employee mailbox created a new forwarding rule or suspicious inbox rule?
  • Do we have a tested process for account takeover response (reset sessions/tokens, review OAuth/app grants, mailbox rules, and sign-in logs)?
  • Which business systems are most at risk from stolen credentials (email, payroll, accounting, CRM), and do we enforce MFA on all of them?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No specific SME-wide patch action is the key takeaway from today’s items. The priority is credential and account protection (MFA enforcement, monitoring of suspicious sign-ins and mailbox rules) and confirming macOS endpoint coverage is in place and working.

One action today

Today, review and remove any unexpected mailbox forwarding/inbox rules on finance and director accounts, and confirm MFA is enforced for those users.
