Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Fortinet VPN credential leak: assume attackers will test logins and target finance teams

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 17 June 2026, 17:03 UK time
Today’s look-out: Stolen VPN credentials and follow-on account takeover / ransomware attempts

What to look out for today

Reports of a new data leak (“FortiBleed”) suggest Fortinet/FortiGate VPN credentials for tens of thousands of devices may be exposed. If your business (or your IT provider) uses Fortinet VPN, assume criminals will try those usernames/passwords at scale to gain remote access.

In parallel, expect a rise in account takeover attempts (phishing, session hijacking, and MFA fatigue prompts) and imposter scams that exploit fear and urgency.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • VPN access is “front door” access. If attackers log in, they can often move quickly to file servers, email, backups and admin tools.
  • SMEs are frequently targeted opportunistically. If your VPN is exposed to the internet, it may be tested automatically whether you’re “big” or not.
  • Finance teams get hit next. Once inside (or once they know who you use), criminals may attempt invoice fraud, payroll diversion, or fake “IT support” calls.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected VPN login alerts, logins at unusual times, or from unusual countries/locations.
  • Repeated MFA prompts reported by staff (“I keep getting approval requests”).
  • New admin accounts, password resets you didn’t request, or changes to security settings.
  • Unusual remote access behaviour: new devices registered, new VPN profiles, or config changes.
  • Sudden requests to change bank details, “urgent” payment approvals, or supplier email tone changes.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Credential stuffing / direct login attempts against Fortinet VPN portals using leaked passwords.
  • Follow-on phishing posing as Fortinet, your IT provider, or “security” teams to trick staff into sharing MFA codes or approving prompts.
  • MFA fatigue: bombarding users with push notifications until someone taps “Approve”.
  • Business email compromise and impersonation to redirect payments once access is gained (or when criminals can convincingly spoof your environment).
  • Ransomware staging after remote access: disabling defences, locating backups, and spreading across shared drives.

What to do today

  • Confirm whether you (or your IT provider) use Fortinet/FortiGate VPN for remote access at any site.
  • If yes: treat VPN credentials as potentially exposed and prioritise a controlled password reset for VPN/remote access users (starting with admins and privileged accounts).
  • Lock down MFA behaviour: ensure users know to report unexpected prompts immediately and never approve an MFA request they didn’t initiate.
  • Review remote access logs for unusual logins and configuration changes; escalate anything suspicious to your IT support.
  • Re-brief finance and ops teams on payment-change verification (call-back to a known number; no changes based only on email).

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have any Fortinet/FortiGate VPN endpoints (including at branch sites) and are they internet-facing?
  • Have you checked VPN authentication logs for unusual access in the last 7–14 days?
  • What’s our plan to rotate VPN/admin credentials safely and quickly (including service accounts, shared accounts, and break-glass access)?
  • Is MFA enforced for all remote access users, and can we reduce MFA fatigue risk (number matching, phishing-resistant options where possible, tighter prompts)?
  • What additional controls are in place (e.g. IP allow-listing, geo-blocking, conditional access, admin access restrictions)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

This is not a “patch list” item, but it is a reminder that any internet-facing VPN or firewall needs a tight maintenance routine. Ask your IT provider to confirm your remote access stack is on supported versions and that emergency security updates are applied promptly, because credential leaks and remote access weaknesses are common starting points for ransomware.

One action today

Ask your IT provider today to confirm whether you use Fortinet/FortiGate VPN and, if you do, to prioritise checking remote-access logs and rotating VPN/admin passwords in a controlled way.
