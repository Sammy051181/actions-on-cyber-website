Today’s SMB cyber lookout: ex‑employee access & insider sabotage risks

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Sunday 14 June 2026, 11:31 UK time
Today’s look-out: Insider risk and ex‑employee access clean-up

What to look out for today

A widely reported case shows how damaging a disgruntled ex-employee can be when they still have (or regain) access to systems. The incident disrupted operations, deleted accounts, and caused significant costs. For SMEs, this is a reminder to treat leavers (including contractors and outsourced IT) as a top cyber risk category.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Smaller organisations often have fewer people, shared logins, informal processes, and “tribal knowledge” in IT. That can make it easier for a former staff member (or contractor) to retain access or know exactly what to target (email, shared drives, finance systems, backups, and key admin accounts). Even a short disruption can stop trading, delay payroll, or knock out customer service.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected account lockouts, password resets, or disabled user accounts.
  • New admin accounts appearing, or admin rights granted “temporarily”.
  • Unexplained deletions in shared drives, mailboxes, or cloud storage.
  • Security alerts about logins at odd hours or from unusual locations/devices.
  • Staff reporting they “can’t access” key systems (MIS/CRM/email/files) without a clear cause.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Using old access: A leaver’s account isn’t fully disabled (or is re-enabled) and is used to delete data or disrupt operations.
  • Leveraging shared credentials: Where teams share passwords, it’s hard to be sure access has been removed.
  • Targeting recovery: Disruption is maximised by damaging admin accounts, documentation, or anything needed to restore systems quickly.

What to do today

  • Run a “leavers access check”: confirm all recent leavers (including temporary staff and suppliers) are removed from email, Microsoft 365/Google, line-of-business apps, remote access, and any admin tools.
  • Stop shared logins where possible: move to named accounts for admin and finance systems, and reset any shared passwords you cannot yet replace.
  • Check admin accounts: review who has admin rights today, and remove anything not needed.
  • Make sure you can recover: confirm backups exist and you know who can restore them (and that this does not rely on a single person).

Ask your IT provider

  • What is our leavers process, and how quickly are accounts disabled across all systems (email, cloud apps, remote access, backups, password manager)?
  • Do you have a list of all privileged/admin accounts, and when were they last reviewed?
  • How do you detect suspicious activity from legitimate accounts (e.g., mass deletions, unusual logins)?
  • Which accounts could delete data or disrupt operations, and do we have extra safeguards (approval steps / alerts) for those actions?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No patch-focused item is included in today’s brief. Today’s priority is access governance: ensuring former staff and suppliers cannot log in, and that admin privileges are tightly controlled and monitored.

One action today

Pick the last three leavers/contractors and confirm—today—that their accounts are disabled everywhere (email, cloud apps, remote access) and that any shared passwords they may have known are reset.
