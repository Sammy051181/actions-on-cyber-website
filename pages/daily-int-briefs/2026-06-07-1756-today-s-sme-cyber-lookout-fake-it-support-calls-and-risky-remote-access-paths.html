Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SME cyber lookout: fake “IT support” calls and risky remote access paths

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Sunday 07 June 2026, 17:56 UK time
Today’s look-out: Social engineering posing as IT support; remote access/device exposure

What to look out for today

Be on guard for phone calls or messages claiming to be “IT support” (or your outsourced IT provider) asking staff to install tools, approve sign-ins, reset passwords, or share files urgently. A ransomware/extortion group is reported to be using fake IT support calls to gain access and steal data quickly, particularly targeting law firms and professional services.

Separately, keep an eye on any unusual internet traffic coming from routers or edge devices. A botnet variant is reported targeting certain router firmware, which can lead to devices being used for malicious activity and business disruption.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Professional services are rich in sensitive data (client documents, contracts, ID, payment details) and are attractive for extortion.
  • Social engineering bypasses “good tech”: attackers target people and processes, not just systems.
  • Edge devices are a single point of failure: if a router is compromised, it can enable further access or cause outages that stop day-to-day work.

Warning signs

  • Unsolicited calls claiming to be IT, your telecoms provider, Microsoft, or a “security team”.
  • Pressure tactics: “within the next 10 minutes”, “we detected a breach”, “your account will be disabled”.
  • Requests to install remote access tools, run “support” apps, or approve MFA prompts you didn’t initiate.
  • Requests for client lists, case files, payroll/HR files, or “a quick export” for troubleshooting.
  • Unexpected router/internet instability, new admin logins, unexplained configuration changes, or spikes in outbound traffic.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation of IT support to convince staff to grant access (remote control, credentials, MFA approval) and move quickly to data theft.
  • Abuse of always-on devices (like routers) to establish a foothold, disrupt operations, or use your connection for unwanted traffic.
  • Targeting “file transfer” and remote access services that are exposed to the internet to crash services or create incidents that distract teams.

What to do today

  • Set a simple rule for staff: never act on inbound “IT support” requests. Hang up and call back using a known number from your internal list.
  • Make MFA push fatigue harder: tell staff to report unexpected prompts immediately and never approve them “to stop them”.
  • Check who can remote in: review remote access tools in use and remove anything not approved.
  • Quick router/edge check: confirm admin access is limited, passwords are unique, and logging/alerts are enabled where possible.
  • Prepare for extortion: confirm you can locate and restore critical data, and that backups are not accessible with everyday user accounts.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have a documented call-back and verification process for any support request?
  • Which remote access tools are approved, and can you provide a list of what’s currently installed?
  • Are our routers/firewalls centrally monitored for unusual outbound traffic and admin logins?
  • What’s our rapid containment plan if a user grants access during a fake support call (disable accounts, revoke sessions, isolate device)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If you run SolarWinds Serv-U (a managed file transfer/file server product), note that a denial-of-service issue is reported as actively exploited. It’s worth checking whether you have it in your environment (including at a supplier/MSP) and ensuring maintenance and monitoring are in place to reduce disruption risk.

One action today

Send a 3-line staff note today: “We will never ask you (inbound call/email) to install support software or approve an MFA prompt. If someone claims to be IT, hang up and call the usual helpdesk number. Report any unexpected MFA prompts immediately.”
