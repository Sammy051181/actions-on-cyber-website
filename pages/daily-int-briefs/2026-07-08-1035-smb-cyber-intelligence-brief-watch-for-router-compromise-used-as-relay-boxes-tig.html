SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: Watch for router compromise used as “relay boxes” + tighten SaaS admin controls

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Wednesday 08 July 2026, 10:35 UK time
Today’s look-out: Compromised routers used as attacker relay infrastructure, and SaaS/chatbot admin-change risk

What to look out for today

Two themes to brief staff and your IT support on today:

  • Internet-facing routers and network devices being compromised and used as “Operational Relay Boxes” (ORBs) to hide attacker activity and support wider intrusions.
  • Cloud/SaaS configuration and admin-rights risk (including customer-facing chatbots): if someone with edit access can change behaviour, they may be able to access conversations or trick users into sharing credentials.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Your router is a dependency: if it’s taken over, attackers can route traffic through you, target you, or create hard-to-trace follow-on access into email, cloud apps, and remote access tools.
  • SMEs often inherit risk from MSPs and “set-and-forget” network kit (old routers, multiple sites, home-working links, temporary firewall rules).
  • Chatbots and automated customer comms are trust channels. If compromised or misconfigured, they can be used for social engineering (“please re-enter your password”), data leakage, and reputational damage.

Warning signs

  • Unexplained internet slowdowns, odd spikes in outbound traffic, or complaints about connectivity that come and go.
  • Router/firewall admin logins you don’t recognise, configuration changes that weren’t requested, or new admin users.
  • New or unexpected port forwards, VPN users, DNS settings, or remote management being enabled.
  • For chatbots/SaaS: unexpected content changes, new “code blocks”/advanced features enabled, or bot responses asking for passwords/one-time codes.
  • Customer reports that your chatbot/site is asking for sensitive details it shouldn’t.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Compromise an exposed network device (often due to missing updates or risky internet exposure) and use it as a relay point to mask attacks on other targets—or as a stepping stone into your environment.
  • Blend in with normal traffic: relay infrastructure can make investigations harder and slow down detection.
  • Abuse legitimate edit rights in cloud tools to change behaviour, access data, or prompt users to share credentials—especially where “advanced” features are enabled.

What to do today

  • Inventory your internet-facing devices: routers, firewalls, VPN concentrators, Wi‑Fi controllers, and anything with remote admin.
  • Confirm remote management is locked down (off if not needed; if needed, restrict by IP/VPN and enforce MFA where supported).
  • Review device and cloud admin accounts: remove stale users, enforce strong authentication, and ensure admin access is least-privilege.
  • For chatbots/customer comms: agree a policy that your bot/support will never ask for passwords or one-time codes; add monitoring/approval for bot content changes.
  • Ensure logs are being collected (router/firewall logs and cloud audit logs) and that someone looks at alerts, not just stores them.

Ask your IT provider

  • Which of our network devices are internet-facing, and who is responsible for monitoring and patching them?
  • Do we have any remote admin enabled on routers/firewalls? If yes, what controls (MFA, IP allow-lists, VPN-only) are in place?
  • How would we detect if our router/firewall was being used as a relay (traffic baselines, anomaly alerts, known-bad destination monitoring)?
  • For any chatbots or customer messaging platforms: who has edit rights, are changes logged, and do we have an approval process for high-risk changes?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

This is a good day to prioritise updates for internet-facing networking equipment and any cloud tools where “advanced” agent/code features are enabled. If you outsource IT, ask for confirmation (in writing) of what was checked and what was updated or risk-accepted.

One action today

Ask your IT support today for a list of all internet-facing routers/firewalls/VPN devices and confirmation that remote management is restricted (or disabled) and admin accounts have been reviewed this month.
