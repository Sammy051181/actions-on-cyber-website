Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SME cyber look-out: “ClickFix” scams that steal Microsoft 365 and browser sessions

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 17 July 2026, 15:01 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing/social-engineering lures that convince staff to paste a command, leading to account takeover and cloud data theft

What to look out for today

Be on alert for messages or webpages that claim a “quick fix” is needed and instruct staff to copy/paste a command into Windows Run, Command Prompt, PowerShell or Terminal to “verify”, “unlock” or “repair” something. This tactic is being used to install infostealer malware that can take saved passwords, live browser session tokens, and data from Microsoft 365 (including synced OneDrive and SharePoint files).

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Account takeover without a password reset: stolen session tokens can let criminals access email and cloud services even if the password is later changed.
  • Business disruption and fraud risk: once inside Microsoft 365, attackers can read invoices, change payment details in email threads, and target finance teams and customers.
  • Fast data loss: synced folders (OneDrive/SharePoint) can be quietly copied out, creating a breach with legal, reputational and client impacts.

Warning signs

  • Any “helpful” instruction that says: Press Win+R, paste this, and hit Enter.
  • Pop-ups or emails claiming your device is “blocked”, “infected”, “out of date”, or that you must “re-authenticate” urgently.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts, sign-in alerts, or mailbox rules you didn’t create (e.g. auto-forwarding, auto-deleting).
  • Staff reporting they were told to run a command to “fix” Microsoft 365, Teams, OneDrive sync, or a browser issue.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Social engineering: attackers pose as IT support, a software vendor, or a ‘verification’ page.
  • Copy/paste execution: the victim runs what looks like a harmless step, but it installs an infostealer.
  • Token and credential theft: the malware takes browser-stored passwords and active sessions.
  • Cloud data grab: attackers pull Microsoft 365 files (including documents in OneDrive/SharePoint) and use email access to expand fraud.

What to do today

  • Tell staff a simple rule: “We never ask you to paste commands from emails or websites to ‘fix’ something.”
  • Harden Microsoft 365 quickly: review sign-in alerts, check for suspicious mailbox rules/forwarding, and confirm MFA is enforced for all users.
  • Reduce token impact: if you suspect someone ran a command, have IT force sign-out and revoke sessions, then reset credentials.
  • Check synced folders: watch for unusual access to SharePoint/OneDrive, bulk downloads, or unfamiliar devices.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have monitoring for mass downloads or unusual access in Microsoft 365 (SharePoint/OneDrive) and alerts for risky sign-ins?
  • Can we quickly revoke user sessions/tokens and force re-authentication if an infostealer is suspected?
  • Are we blocking or warning on common “run this command” behaviours (e.g. suspicious script execution) on endpoints?
  • Do we routinely check for mailbox auto-forwarding rules and other persistence tricks?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No major SME-wide patch emergency is highlighted in today’s items. Focus today on staff awareness and identity/session protection, as this threat relies more on social engineering than a traditional software flaw.

One action today

Send a same-day internal note: “Do not paste commands from emails/websites into Run/Terminal. If prompted, stop and call IT.”
