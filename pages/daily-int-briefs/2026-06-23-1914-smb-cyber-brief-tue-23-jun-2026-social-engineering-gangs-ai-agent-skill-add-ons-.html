SMB Cyber Brief (Tue 23 Jun 2026): Social engineering gangs + “AI agent/skill” add‑ons = higher supplier & account takeover risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Tuesday 23 June 2026, 19:14 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier impersonation & account takeover, plus risky AI/automation add‑ons and developer supply chain controls

What to look out for today

Two themes to brief your team on today:

  • Social engineering and account takeover tactics used by groups like Scattered Spider (the same style of attacks often targets finance teams, helpdesks and outsourced IT).
  • Third-party add-ons and “AI agent/skill” marketplaces that can quietly collect data or gain access even when they appear to pass security checks.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • SMEs are often easier to trick than to hack: a convincing call to the helpdesk, a fake supplier email, or a rushed “urgent” request can lead to email takeover or fraudulent payments.
  • AI assistants/agents and marketplace “skills” are increasingly being adopted by teams without formal security review, creating a new supplier/data-sharing route.
  • If you rely on an outsourced IT provider or MSP, you need clarity on how they verify identity for password/MFA resets and how they approve new integrations.

Warning signs

  • Helpdesk/IT requests that try to bypass normal steps: “I’ve got a new phone”, “I’m locked out”, “can you reset MFA urgently”.
  • Supplier/bank detail ‘changes’ arriving right before payroll, month-end, or an invoice due date.
  • New AI tools or add-ons being connected to Microsoft 365/Google Workspace/CRM with broad permissions “to work properly”.
  • Unexpected prompts asking staff to approve sign-ins, OAuth consent screens, or “authorise this app”.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation + pressure: attackers pose as staff, suppliers, or even your IT provider to trigger password resets, MFA changes, mailbox access, or invoice redirection.
  • Quiet data capture via add-ons: a seemingly harmless AI “skill” can request access and collect basic identifiers (like email addresses) at scale; a more malicious one could seek broader access if users approve it.
  • Developer supply-chain angle (for firms that build/host code): attackers try to get code or workflow changes merged via pull requests, then abuse automation to access secrets or systems. GitHub is tightening defaults/behaviour in this area, but SMEs still need to review how their workflows are configured.

What to do today

  • Run a 10-minute “verify before you act” reminder to finance and admin staff: bank detail changes and urgent payment requests must be confirmed using a known phone number (not one in the email).
  • Lock down who can add integrations to your email/CRM/helpdesk/automation tools; remove unused apps and review any newly approved apps.
  • Re-check your helpdesk identity process (internal or outsourced): require a call-back to a known number and a second factor for any MFA reset/device change.
  • If you use AI assistants/agents: make a simple rule—no new “skills/plugins/connectors” without owner approval and a quick permission review.

Ask your IT provider

  • What is your step-by-step identity verification process for password resets and MFA/device changes (especially for directors and finance)?
  • Do we have conditional access / sign-in rules to reduce account takeover risk (e.g., blocking risky locations, requiring MFA consistently)?
  • How do you monitor and alert on suspicious mailbox rules, forwarding, and new app consents in Microsoft 365/Google?
  • Do we have a register of approved SaaS integrations (including AI tools), and can you provide a monthly list of newly added apps/permissions?
  • If we use GitHub: have you checked our workflows for risky automation triggers and confirmed we’re using the safer, up-to-date approach for code checkout?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Industrial/operational technology users should note Siemens ICS advisories released today. If you run Siemens SINEC INS or SIPROTEC 5 in facilities, manufacturing, or building/energy environments, confirm with your maintainer/IT provider that you’re on supported versions and have vendor-recommended mitigations in place.

One action today

Send a same-day message to finance/admin and your IT helpdesk: “No bank detail changes or MFA resets without call-back to a known number and a second verification step.”
