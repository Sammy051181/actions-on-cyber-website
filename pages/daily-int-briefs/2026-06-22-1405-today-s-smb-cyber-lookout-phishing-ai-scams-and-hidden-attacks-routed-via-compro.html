Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: phishing + AI scams, and “hidden” attacks routed via compromised routers

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Monday 22 June 2026, 14:05 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing and AI-enabled scams, plus attacks masked through compromised routers/proxy networks

What to look out for today

Expect more convincing phishing and “AI-assisted” scam messages (invoices, payroll, banking, delivery and supplier themes), and be aware that some attacks may appear to come from “normal” looking internet locations because criminals are increasingly routing activity through compromised routers acting as proxy infrastructure.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Phishing remains the most common way criminals get into Microsoft 365/Google accounts, steal money, and set up invoice fraud.
  • AI makes scams quicker to produce and harder to spot (better grammar, more personalisation, more believable back-and-forth).
  • Proxy networks built from compromised routers can help criminals hide where they are really operating from, making fraud and account takeovers harder to detect and block.

Warning signs

  • “Urgent” payment, bank detail, or payee changes—especially when sent near month-end, payroll cut-off, or just before a weekend.
  • Messages that move you from email to another channel quickly (WhatsApp/Signal/phone) to “confirm” bank details.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts (“Approve sign-in?”) or staff reporting repeated login codes they didn’t request.
  • Supplier emails that look right but feel slightly “off”: new wording, new tone, new account number, or requests to bypass normal approval steps.
  • Unusual sign-ins to cloud accounts from unfamiliar locations or at odd times (even if they don’t look obviously ‘foreign’).

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Phishing for cloud credentials to access email, then using mailbox rules to hide replies and intercept invoices.
  • Business email compromise (BEC) to request fraudulent payments and “change of bank details” scams.
  • Using proxy networks (built from compromised routers) to disguise logins, reduce reputation-based blocking, and blend into normal traffic patterns.
  • Follow-on ransomware or data theft after an initial account compromise (e.g., using stolen access to pivot into devices, shared drives, or third-party tools).

What to do today

  • Re-brief staff (finance, admin, reception, payroll): no bank detail changes without an out-of-band call to a known number already on file.
  • Turn “MFA fatigue” into an incident trigger: if someone gets unexpected prompts, they must report it immediately and not approve anything.
  • Check your cloud sign-in alerts (Microsoft 365/Google): ensure alerts are enabled for suspicious sign-ins and mailbox rule creation.
  • Review router/Wi‑Fi ownership: confirm who manages your internet router (ISP vs IT provider) and whether it’s business-grade and supported.

Ask your IT provider

  • Are we alerting on impossible travel, repeated failed logins, and new inbox rules/forwarding in Microsoft 365/Google?
  • Do we have a documented process for payment change verification (and have you tested it with our finance team)?
  • Who is responsible for our router and firewall (patching, admin access, remote management settings, and replacement if end-of-life)?
  • Are we using controls that reduce BEC impact (e.g., anti-impersonation protections, stricter DMARC handling, and safer external email tagging)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

This is a good week to confirm your internet router/firewall isn’t an old, unsupported model and that it’s being maintained by someone accountable. The risk theme isn’t a single bug; it’s that legacy routers are increasingly repurposed by criminals as stealthy infrastructure for wider attacks.

One action today

Send a 3-line reminder to finance and payroll today: no bank detail/payment instruction changes without calling a known number on file, and treat unexpected MFA prompts as an immediate security incident.
