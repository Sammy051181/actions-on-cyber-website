Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Supplier breach follow‑up scams: watch for fake “security notices” and account resets

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Sunday 10 May 2026, 11:49 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier incident scams and security-software trust risk

What to look out for today

Be alert for phishing and social engineering that references well-known suppliers and “urgent security updates”. Two stories are likely to be reused by criminals in convincing messages:

  • A confirmed user data exposure affecting NVIDIA GeForce NOW users in Armenia.
  • A claimed breach involving Trellix source code (a security vendor), which can trigger “trust panic” and fake support outreach.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Even if your organisation doesn’t use these specific services, attackers commonly recycle widely reported incidents to:

  • Pressure staff into clicking “reset your password” links or opening “incident reports”.
  • Impersonate IT support or suppliers to obtain MFA codes, remote access, or payment details.
  • Create confusion about which security tools can be trusted, prompting risky changes made in a hurry.

Warning signs

  • Email or Teams/Slack message claiming you’re “affected” and must act today, especially with a link to “verify” or “re-secure” your account.
  • Unexpected password reset or MFA prompts you didn’t initiate.
  • Calls from “security support” pushing you to install remote-control software or share one-time codes.
  • Messages that use brand names (NVIDIA, Trellix) but come from lookalike domains or personal email addresses.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Incident-themed phishing: “We detected your account in the breach—confirm details here.”
  • Fake support / helpdesk scams: attackers claim they can “secure your endpoint” and request MFA codes or remote access.
  • Credential stuffing and account takeovers: if any exposed data is reused elsewhere, criminals try the same logins on common business services.

What to do today

  • Remind staff: don’t click security links from unexpected messages—go via your normal bookmarked login pages.
  • Finance/admin teams: treat any “supplier security incident” email as a red flag for payment-change scams; verify using a known phone number.
  • Check your key accounts (email, Microsoft 365/Google, finance tools): ensure MFA is enabled and recovery details are correct.
  • If you use Trellix or any managed security service: confirm how your provider validates updates and handles vendor security advisories.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have a process for verifying supplier/security-vendor notices (and stopping staff acting on fakes)?
  • How are suspicious MFA prompts and sign-in anomalies monitored and handled?
  • If a major vendor is breached, what’s our standard response (review access, rotate credentials, check logs, communications to staff)?
  • Do we block common remote-support tools by default, or at least require approval?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your organisation (or your web/hosting supplier) uses cPanel/WHM, confirm updates are being applied promptly. Hosting control panels are high-value targets, so you want clear ownership: who patches it, how quickly, and how you’re told if it impacts availability.

One action today

Send a same-day internal note: “Don’t act on breach/security emails from suppliers—log in via bookmarks and verify any request by calling a known number.”
