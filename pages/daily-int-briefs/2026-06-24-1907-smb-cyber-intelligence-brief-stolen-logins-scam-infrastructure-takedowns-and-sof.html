SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: Stolen logins, scam infrastructure takedowns, and software supply-chain risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 24 June 2026, 19:07 UK time
Today’s look-out: Credential-theft ripple effects + supplier/supply-chain compromise leading to account takeover and payment fraud

What to look out for today

  • Stolen password fallout: Law enforcement disrupted malware infrastructure and recovered a very large cache of stolen credentials. Expect increased account takeover attempts (email, Microsoft 365/Google, payroll, bookkeeping, CRM) using previously-stolen logins.
  • Payment and invoicing scams: Credential theft commonly fuels Business Email Compromise (BEC) and fake bank-detail changes.
  • Supplier and software supply-chain risk: Researchers flagged a CI/CD workflow weakness pattern that can allow attackers to hijack development workflows and compromise code in repositories—this can ripple into third-party software and updates you rely on.
  • Scam operations and money movement: US authorities seized a cloud account allegedly tied to scam money laundering—this is a reminder that scam ecosystems are well-resourced and cross-border, and will keep targeting victims via email, SMS and social channels.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs are attractive targets because a single compromised mailbox or admin account can quickly lead to: diverted payments, payroll fraud, fraudulent supplier onboarding, theft of customer data, and ransomware. You may not be directly targeted by “big” actors, but you can be hit by automated login attempts using stolen credentials, or by scams that leverage compromised supplier accounts.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected MFA prompts or “approve sign-in” notifications.
  • Multiple failed login alerts from unfamiliar locations, or “impossible travel” sign-ins.
  • New inbox rules/forwarding set up (e.g., copies of invoices sent externally).
  • Supplier emails that suddenly change tone, add urgency, or request bank detail changes.
  • New “app consent” / third-party app access granted in Microsoft 365/Google without a clear business reason.
  • Invoices re-sent with slightly changed bank details, or requests to “pay this time to a different account”.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Credential stuffing: Attackers try stolen username/password pairs across Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, payroll and finance platforms.
  • Mailbox takeover → BEC: Once in, they watch email threads and insert themselves into real conversations to redirect payments.
  • Supply-chain compromise: If a vendor’s repository/workflows are hijacked, attackers may slip malicious changes into software, updates or deployment pipelines—creating downstream risk for customers.
  • Scam “cash-out” routes: Mature scam groups use cloud infrastructure and laundering networks to move stolen funds quickly, reducing the time you have to recover money.

What to do today

  • Finance check: Re-brief staff: bank-detail changes must be verified via a known phone number (not the one in the email). Add a two-person approval for new payees and changes.
  • Email account controls: Ensure MFA is on for all users, and use phishing-resistant MFA where possible for admins.
  • Quick mailbox hygiene: Review forwarding rules, suspicious inbox rules, delegated access and newly-added OAuth/app integrations.
  • Password reuse audit: If staff reuse passwords between work and personal sites, assume higher risk. Encourage a password manager and unique passwords for key accounts.
  • Supplier comms: Remind key suppliers (bookkeeping/payroll/IT/logistics) you will verify payment changes out-of-band—set expectations now.

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you show us a report of risky sign-ins, legacy authentication blocks, and MFA coverage for all users and admins?
  • Do we have alerts for new mailbox forwarding rules, unusual inbox rules, and new OAuth/app consents?
  • What’s our process for responding to a suspected mailbox takeover (containment, password reset, token revocation, rule cleanup, customer/supplier comms)?
  • Which key vendors/software tools we rely on have strong software supply-chain controls (code signing, protected branches, CI/CD hardening), and how do we monitor vendor security advisories?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Threat intelligence reporting highlighted real-world exploitation of a Cisco SD-WAN Manager zero-day earlier this year. If you (or your IT provider) run SD-WAN/edge networking platforms, confirm who owns patching and monitoring for those systems—these devices can be business-critical and a high-impact foothold when compromised.

One action today

Run a same-day check for suspicious email forwarding/inbox rules and new third-party app access in your email platform, and remove anything not explicitly approved.
