Today’s SMB cyber lookout: scam follow‑ups after breaches, and smarter social engineering on messaging & mobiles

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Tuesday 12 May 2026, 22:08 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier/brand breach follow‑up scams and mobile banking scam calls

What to look out for today

Be alert for “breach follow‑up” scams and payment/account takeover attempts that piggyback on widely reported incidents and popular apps:

  • Messages pretending to be from a well-known supplier/brand about a “security incident”, “refund”, “compensation”, or “account verification”.
  • Social engineering in messaging apps (including Signal) designed to trick staff into approving prompts, sharing codes, or moving a conversation off official channels.
  • Banking scam calls and texts targeting Android users, pressuring them to “secure” accounts or install/update something urgently.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Brand trust is a weapon: when a big name reports a breach, criminals quickly use it to make phishing feel believable.
  • Finance teams are the prize: scam follow-ups often aim for invoice redirection, bank detail changes, and card/payment fraud.
  • Mobiles are business endpoints: if staff use personal or work Android phones for banking, email, Microsoft 365/Google Workspace, or approvals, a successful scam can lead to fast account takeover.
  • Regulatory pressure is real: UK enforcement action shows that poor security can result in significant cost and reputational damage.

Warning signs

  • Email/SMS/WhatsApp/Signal message saying your account was affected by a breach and you must act “within 24 hours”.
  • Links to “verify”, “reset password”, “download a security update”, or “claim compensation/refund”.
  • Requests to share one-time passcodes, QR codes, backup codes, or to approve a login/pairing prompt you didn’t initiate.
  • Phone calls that pressure staff to move money, change supplier bank details, or “confirm” bank login information.
  • Unexpected changes in an online shop/supplier portal experience (new login prompts, unusual MFA requests, sudden password resets you didn’t request).

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation: pretending to be a breached organisation (or a “claims handler”) to harvest logins and payment details.
  • Conversation hijack: using messaging apps to build trust, then pushing victims to approve prompts or share codes.
  • Payment diversion: sending “updated bank details” for a supplier, or a fake refund that becomes a payment request.
  • Mobile-first account takeover: steering staff into actions on their phone (calls/texts) where they’re more likely to tap through prompts quickly.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): “Breach news = scam wave”. Don’t click links in breach/refund messages—go direct to the organisation’s website or known portal.
  • Harden payment change controls: require a call-back to a known number (from your records, not the email) before changing any bank details.
  • Reinforce MFA rules: if someone receives an MFA prompt they didn’t start, treat it as a compromised password and report immediately.
  • Mobile hygiene: remind staff not to install apps/updates from links in messages; use official app stores and trusted settings only.
  • Check your comms channels: confirm which channels your organisation uses for finance requests and ensure staff know “we never ask for codes over chat”.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have phishing-resistant MFA options available for key accounts (email, finance, admin)? If not, what’s the roadmap?
  • Can you enable/confirm alerting for suspicious logins (new device, impossible travel, repeated MFA prompts) for Microsoft 365/Google and finance apps?
  • What’s our process for rapid credential reset if someone approves an unexpected prompt or enters a password on a fake site?
  • Do we have mobile device controls (basic MDM, screen lock, OS update compliance) for staff who access business email/approvals on phones?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Nothing specific to rush from today’s items, but it’s a good time to verify that phones and messaging apps used for work are set to auto-update and that staff are on supported versions—many scam protections rely on current OS/app features.

One action today

Send a same-day reminder to all staff: don’t click “breach/refund/security alert” links—go direct to the official site/portal, and report any unexpected MFA prompts immediately.
