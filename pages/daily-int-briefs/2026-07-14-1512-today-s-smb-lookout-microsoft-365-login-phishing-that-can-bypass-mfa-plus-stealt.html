Today’s SMB lookout: Microsoft 365 login phishing that can bypass MFA (plus stealthy Entra credential checks)

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Tuesday 14 July 2026, 15:12 UK time
Today’s look-out: Microsoft 365 / Entra ID account takeover via phishing and stealthy credential validation

What to look out for today

Be on heightened alert for phishing aimed at Microsoft 365 users (Outlook/Teams/SharePoint/OneDrive logins) that can defeat multi-factor authentication (MFA), plus newer cloud techniques that can quietly validate stolen credentials in Microsoft Entra ID without obvious “successful sign-in” signals.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Microsoft 365 is the front door for many SMEs. If one mailbox or Teams account is taken over, attackers often use it to phish your customers and suppliers from a trusted address.
  • Finance and payroll fraud risk increases quickly once an attacker can read emails, monitor invoices, or set up forwarding rules.
  • MFA is not a silver bullet if staff can be tricked into approving prompts or handing over session access via realistic login pages.

Warning signs

  • Staff report being asked to re-authenticate to view a file, voicemail, Teams message, or “secure document”, especially if the link goes to an unexpected domain.
  • MFA prompts the user didn’t initiate (or repeated prompts), followed by account lockouts.
  • Unusual email behaviour: new inbox rules, auto-forwarding, “deleted items” filling up, or sent messages the user didn’t send.
  • Supplier/customer reports of odd replies from your staff asking to “confirm payment details” or “re-send an invoice”.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Convincing Microsoft 365 phishing pages designed to capture credentials and bypass MFA protections.
  • Account reconnaissance and credential checking in Entra ID environments in ways that may not look like a normal login success, making it easier to test stolen username/password pairs without triggering the usual alarms.
  • Once inside: set mailbox forwarding, create inbox rules to hide replies, then attempt invoice redirection or payroll diversion.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff warning: don’t log in via links in emails/texts; go via bookmarks or the official Microsoft portal/app.
  • Ask your admin/IT to check for risky mailbox rules and auto-forwarding across M365, prioritising finance, senior leadership, and shared mailboxes.
  • Make sure staff know what to do if they get unexpected MFA prompts: deny, then report immediately.
  • For payment changes: enforce a call-back verification (using a known number, not one in the email) before changing bank details.

Ask your IT provider

  • How are we detecting and alerting on suspicious Entra/M365 activity beyond “successful sign-ins” (e.g., unusual patterns, risky users, suspicious app activity)?
  • Do we have a standard process to audit and remove malicious inbox rules/forwarding quickly after a suspected compromise?
  • Which users are protected with stronger phishing-resistant sign-in options (and is there a plan to expand that coverage)?
  • If a mailbox is compromised, what’s our first-hour playbook (reset, revoke sessions, rule cleanup, customer/supplier comms)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Separate to the phishing risk, SAP has issued security updates across multiple products this month. If you run SAP in-house or via a partner, confirm who is responsible for maintenance and how quickly critical vendor updates are assessed and applied.

One action today

Email staff today: “Don’t sign in to Microsoft 365 from email links—use your bookmark/app; deny unexpected MFA prompts and report them immediately.”
