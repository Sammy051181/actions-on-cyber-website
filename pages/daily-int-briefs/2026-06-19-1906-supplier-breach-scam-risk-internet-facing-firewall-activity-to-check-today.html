Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Supplier breach scam risk + internet‑facing firewall activity to check today

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 19 June 2026, 19:06 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier incident scams, firewall exposure, and website/checkout third‑party risk

What to look out for today

Three practical themes to brief your team on today:

  • Supplier breach ripple effects: expect impersonation emails and calls referencing a real incident at a vendor.
  • Internet-facing firewall activity: if you use Fortinet FortiGate (or your IT provider does on your behalf), confirm you’re not exposed to ongoing malicious activity.
  • Website and checkout risks: malware on legitimate sites and third-party scripts on checkout pages can lead to credential theft, card fraud, and follow-on ransomware.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Third-party incidents become phishing fuel: attackers often use real breach news to make payment-change scams and “reset your account” emails feel believable.
  • Single points of failure: an exposed edge device (firewall/VPN) can be a fast route to business disruption, even if the rest of IT is well-managed.
  • Online revenue and trust: if you take payments online, what runs in customers’ browsers (tags, widgets, analytics, payment frames) can materially affect compliance and fraud risk.

Warning signs

  • Emails/calls claiming to be from a licensing, membership, government or service provider saying your details were exposed and you must “verify” or “reconfirm” urgently.
  • Payment details changes (new bank account, new payee, “temporary account”) tied to an alleged incident or system outage.
  • Unexpected password reset prompts or MFA requests you didn’t initiate.
  • Website customers report odd redirects, “your device is infected” pop-ups, or unusual checkout behaviour.
  • Staff report new browser add-ons appearing or being prompted to “install a security/update tool” to view a document.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation off the back of breach news: criminals reference a real supplier incident to push invoice fraud, account takeover attempts, or data-harvesting forms.
  • Targeting exposed edge devices: attackers focus on internet-accessible firewalls/VPNs where a single foothold can lead to internal access and ransomware.
  • “Trust chaining” via legitimate websites: people are more likely to click when the site is real (e.g., a compromised WordPress site), which can be used to deliver malware or steal session cookies.
  • Checkout script abuse: third-party scripts/tags can be abused to skim or manipulate what customers see during payment, creating fraud and reputational damage.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff note: “No bank detail changes by email. Verify using a known number. Don’t approve unexpected MFA prompts.”
  • Finance control: enforce call-back verification for any supplier payment change, and require a second approver.
  • Website hygiene: review who can install plugins/tags, remove anything unused, and make sure admin accounts have MFA.
  • Edge device check: confirm your firewall/VPN management is monitored, not exposed unnecessarily, and that logs/alerts are being reviewed.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we (or you on our behalf) run Fortinet FortiGate anywhere? If yes, have you confirmed we’re not affected by the ongoing activity CISA is warning about, and what evidence do you have (monitoring/log review)?
  • What’s our process for detecting unusual sign-ins and impossible travel, and who gets alerted?
  • For our website: who has access to the CMS, plugins, and tag manager? Do we have a monthly review of third-party scripts and accounts?
  • If a supplier incident triggers a wave of phishing, what’s our rapid comms plan to staff and finance?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

CISA has urged Fortinet customers to take steps due to ongoing malicious activity aimed at internet-accessible FortiGate devices. Even if your IT is outsourced, ask for written confirmation that your perimeter devices are checked and monitored, and that exposure to the internet is minimised.

One action today

Email finance and admin teams today: “No supplier bank-detail changes by email—always verify using a known phone number and require two-person approval.”
