SMB Cyber Briefing (Fri 12 June 2026): Software supply-chain risk (Arch Linux AUR) + actively exploited PeopleSoft issue

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 12 June 2026, 19:19 UK time
Today’s look-out: Software supply-chain compromise and credential theft (developer/IT tooling) + HR/payroll platform risk via PeopleSoft

What to look out for today

  • Software supply-chain compromise (Linux tooling): reports that 400+ Arch User Repository (AUR) packages were compromised to push a rootkit and infostealer designed to steal credentials and access tokens.
  • HR/payroll/ERP platform risk (PeopleSoft): CISA has added an Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools issue to its catalogue of known exploited vulnerabilities, indicating active exploitation is happening in the wild.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Credentials are the prize: infostealers typically target saved passwords, browser sessions and access tokens that can let attackers into email, cloud apps, code repos, finance platforms and admin portals.
  • Supply chain hits SMEs indirectly: even if you don’t run Arch Linux, an outsourced IT provider, web agency, developer, or contractor might use it – and stolen credentials can be used to access your systems.
  • PeopleSoft is often a dependency: you may use PeopleSoft directly, or it may sit behind an outsourced payroll/HR/ERP service – meaning disruption, account compromise or data access could become your problem quickly.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected prompts for logins or MFA re-approval in email/cloud apps, especially from IT/dev/admin accounts.
  • New device logins, unusual locations, or impossible travel alerts in Microsoft 365/Google/other SaaS.
  • New API keys/tokens created, OAuth app consent granted unexpectedly, or unfamiliar integrations appearing in SaaS admin consoles.
  • IT provider reports of a developer workstation rebuild, “we found an infostealer”, or sudden credential resets across multiple services.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Trojaned packages on a workstation/server can install an infostealer to harvest browser sessions, SSH keys, passwords and cloud tokens.
  • Stolen credentials/tokens are then used for account takeover (email, file storage, password managers, finance tools), leading to invoice fraud, data theft, or ransomware deployment.
  • Where organisations rely on PeopleSoft/PeopleTools, attackers may attempt to exploit exposed or poorly protected instances to gain unauthorised access and pivot further.

What to do today

  • Ask internally and with suppliers: do any staff, contractors or your IT provider use Arch Linux + AUR on machines that can access your systems?
  • Prioritise credential hygiene: for admins/finance/IT, enforce MFA, rotate passwords where reuse is suspected, and revoke active sessions/tokens where your SaaS supports it.
  • Review privileged access: check who has admin roles in Microsoft 365/Google/finance platforms; remove stale accounts and apply least privilege.
  • Heighten monitoring for 48–72 hours: focus on new inbox rules/forwarding, new OAuth consents, and new admin accounts.
  • If you use PeopleSoft (directly or via a provider): confirm your PeopleSoft environment is inventoried, monitored, and that your supplier has acted on the actively exploited issue.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do any managed endpoints (or contractor endpoints) use Arch Linux and AUR, and if so what controls are in place to prevent/spot malicious packages?
  • Do you have detection in place for infostealer behaviour and for unusual browser-token/session activity on endpoints?
  • Can you show us a quick check of recent suspicious sign-ins and new OAuth app consents for our Microsoft 365/Google tenancy?
  • Do we (or any of our key suppliers) run Oracle PeopleSoft/PeopleTools, and what is the status of remediation/mitigations given CISA says it is actively exploited?
  • What is our “blast radius” if a developer/admin laptop is compromised – are admin actions separated, and are privileged accounts protected?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

CISA has flagged an actively exploited Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools vulnerability (CVE-2026-35273). If you use PeopleSoft (or a supplier uses it for your HR/payroll/ERP), treat this as urgent: confirm ownership, exposure, monitoring, and rapid remediation with your IT team/provider.

One action today

Check your admin and finance accounts for unusual sign-ins and revoke/rotate credentials and active sessions where anything looks suspicious (especially for any users with elevated access).
