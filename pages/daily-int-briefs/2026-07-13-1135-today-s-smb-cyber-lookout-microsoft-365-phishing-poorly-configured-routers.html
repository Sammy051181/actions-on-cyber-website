Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: Microsoft 365 phishing + poorly configured routers

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Monday 13 July 2026, 11:35 UK time
Today’s look-out: Email account takeover and network entry via weak router configuration

What to look out for today

Two themes to brief staff and your IT support on today:

  • Microsoft 365 phishing aimed at stealing logins and session access (so attackers can get into mailboxes even when passwords are changed).
  • Attackers targeting poorly configured internet routers to gain a foothold, especially where remote management is exposed or admin settings are weak.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Email takeover is a fast route to money loss: intercepted invoices, “change of bank details” requests, and payroll/expense fraud.
  • Routers are a single point of failure: if the edge device is compromised, attackers may watch traffic, access internal systems, or disrupt connectivity.
  • Outsourced IT and MSP dependency risk: if router management is shared across many customers, a repeated misconfiguration can create widespread exposure.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected Microsoft 365 sign-in prompts or repeated MFA requests you didn’t initiate (“MFA fatigue”).
  • Emails in your sent items you don’t recognise, new mailbox rules/forwarding you didn’t set, or replies going missing.
  • Suppliers/customers receiving odd replies from your staff that “sound like them” but request urgent payment changes.
  • Internet dropping out, router reboots, configuration changes you can’t explain, or new admin accounts on the router.
  • Remote access tools or VPN accounts appearing that no one in the business requested.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Microsoft 365 phishing kits are designed to capture credentials and bypass common controls by tricking users into approving a sign-in or handing over session access.
  • Router compromise can be used as a stepping stone to scan your internal network, capture credentials, or quietly maintain access while attackers target email, file shares, or cloud admin portals.
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC) often follows: attackers watch email threads, then strike at the right moment with a realistic payment diversion request.

What to do today

  • Send a short staff alert: treat Microsoft 365 login pages and MFA prompts with caution; report unexpected prompts immediately.
  • Check Microsoft 365 account hygiene: review mailbox forwarding, inbox rules, and sign-in activity for key roles (finance, payroll, directors, shared mailboxes).
  • Lock down payment changes: enforce “call-back on known numbers” for any bank detail change; don’t rely on email-only approval.
  • Router quick check: confirm remote management is not exposed to the internet unless absolutely required; ensure admin access uses strong unique credentials and MFA where available.
  • Prioritise finance and admin accounts for stronger protections (MFA, conditional access, reduced admin rights).

Ask your IT provider

  • Which routers/firewalls do we have, who manages them, and is remote administration enabled? If yes, how is it restricted?
  • Do we have a baseline configuration standard (unique admin creds, MFA where supported, logging, backups of config)?
  • How do you monitor for Microsoft 365 mailbox rule abuse and suspicious sign-ins for our tenant?
  • If a mailbox is taken over, what is the one-hour response plan (disable sessions, reset credentials, check rules/forwarding, notify finance, contact bank)?
  • Are our key accounts (finance/payroll/admin) protected with stronger controls than standard users?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

There are reports of exploited vulnerabilities affecting certain Joomla extensions; if you run a Joomla website, ask your web support to confirm whether those extensions are in use and whether the site is being actively monitored for unexpected admin changes or suspicious new files.

One action today

Today, have finance and IT jointly review Microsoft 365 sign-in activity and mailbox forwarding/rules for all finance, payroll and director accounts, and block any email-only bank detail change requests.
