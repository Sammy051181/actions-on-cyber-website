SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: Microsoft 365 “device code” phishing, spoofed calls, and supplier-breach fallout

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Monday 13 July 2026, 16:10 UK time
Today’s look-out: Account takeover via Microsoft 365 phishing + phone-led impersonation scams + supplier breach follow-on emails

What to look out for today

Three themes SMEs should brief staff on today:

  • Microsoft 365 login phishing designed to steal sessions (not just passwords) using “device code” style prompts and man-in-the-middle techniques.
  • Caller ID spoofing and phone-led impersonation (fake “bank”, “IT support”, “HMRC”, “supplier”, or “Microsoft” calls) continuing despite law enforcement action.
  • Supplier/service-provider breach ripple effects: expect “we need you to re-verify” or “download updated invoice/order” emails that reference a known brand incident.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Microsoft 365 account takeover can quickly lead to invoice fraud, payroll diversion, mailbox rule changes, and convincing internal/external phishing from your real address.
  • Phone + email combinations (spoofed call followed by an email/link) are effective against busy finance and admin teams.
  • Supplier breaches are often used as a pretext for follow-on scams against customers, even if your own systems weren’t breached.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected prompts to “sign in using a device code” or to approve a sign-in on another device.
  • Login pages that feel “normal”, but the user is asked to keep a window open, re-authenticate repeatedly, or approve unusual prompts.
  • Calls that appear to come from a trusted number/brand, insisting on urgency (payment release, account lockout, “security incident”).
  • Emails referencing a supplier breach or “security update” and pushing you to click a link, open an attachment, or provide login details.
  • Sudden mailbox oddities: missing emails, new forwarding rules, or “seen” messages staff swear they didn’t read.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Session theft/account takeover: phishing kits can capture authentication in a way that bypasses basic password checks, then use the mailbox for internal reconnaissance and fraud.
  • Business email compromise follow-through: attackers monitor invoice/payment threads and then request a “bank change” at the perfect moment.
  • Impersonation by phone: spoofed caller ID makes a scam call look legitimate; the caller then directs the victim to a “verification” step (often a link or code).
  • Supplier-incident pretexting: criminals use real news of a service-provider breach to make their emails sound credible.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): “No one should ever read out login codes, approve unexpected sign-in prompts, or follow a device-code login request from an email/call.”
  • Finance control: enforce a call-back process for any bank detail change or urgent payment request using a known-good number from your own records (not the email signature).
  • Mailbox safety check: have admins review high-risk mailboxes (finance, payroll, directors) for suspicious forwarding rules and unexpected sign-ins.
  • External comms: remind customers/suppliers how you will (and won’t) request payment changes.

Ask your IT provider

  • Are we monitoring Microsoft 365 for unusual sign-ins and alerts that indicate possible session-based account takeover?
  • Do we have conditional access or equivalent controls to reduce risky sign-ins (e.g., impossible travel, unusual locations) for key mailboxes?
  • Can you confirm we regularly review (or alert on) new inbox forwarding rules and suspicious mailbox access?
  • What is our invoice fraud runbook (who to contact, what to freeze, what to preserve) if a mailbox is suspected compromised?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No specific patch-driven issue is the main driver today. The immediate risk is credential/session theft and impersonation, so prioritise sign-in monitoring, strong authentication controls, and anti-fraud processes over ad-hoc patch activity.

One action today

Run a same-day “payment change” drill: remind staff to reject any bank detail change unless verified via a call-back to a known number, then document who approves exceptions.
