Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Fortinet firewall/VPN credential leak: check remote access and reset where needed

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 19 June 2026, 12:41 UK time
Today’s look-out: Remote access compromise risk (firewall/VPN credential leak)

What to look out for today

Reports of leaked credentials tied to a large number of internet-accessible Fortinet devices (firewalls and SSL VPN gateways). If your business (or your IT provider) uses Fortinet for remote access, assume attackers will try these leaked logins to gain entry.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Firewalls and VPNs sit at the front door of your network. If an attacker can log in, they may be able to:

  • Access email, file shares and finance systems via your internal network
  • Steal data for extortion
  • Deploy ransomware and disrupt operations
  • Create new admin users or persistence so they can return later

This is particularly relevant for SMEs that rely on always-on SSL VPN for staff, outsourced IT support, or third-party suppliers.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected or out-of-hours VPN logins, especially from unusual locations
  • Multiple failed login attempts followed by a success
  • New admin accounts, new VPN users, or configuration changes no one requested
  • Users reporting MFA prompts they did not trigger (if MFA is used)
  • Sudden account lockouts or password reset emails for VPN/admin accounts

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • “Credential stuffing” against your VPN/admin portal using leaked usernames/passwords
  • Logging in as a legitimate user to blend in and avoid detection
  • Pivoting from VPN access to servers, backups and shared drives
  • Using remote access as a stepping stone to deploy ransomware

What to do today

  • Confirm whether you use Fortinet SSL VPN or FortiGate internet-facing admin access (many SMEs do via an IT provider).
  • Reset VPN and admin passwords and terminate active sessions where appropriate (don’t assume ‘we already changed one password’ is enough).
  • Ensure MFA is enabled for VPN and any administrative access (and check it’s enforced for all accounts).
  • Review access logs for the last few weeks for unusual logins and configuration changes.
  • Limit exposure: if you don’t need SSL VPN, consider disabling it or restricting it (e.g., by IP allow-lists) in line with your IT provider’s guidance.
  • Be ready for incident response: confirm you can restore critical systems and that backups are protected from admin/VPN compromise.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have any internet-accessible Fortinet devices (FortiGate / SSL VPN) and are any potentially affected by the credential exposure reports?
  • Have you terminated active VPN/admin sessions and reset all relevant credentials?
  • Is MFA enforced for every VPN user and every admin account (no exceptions)?
  • Have you checked for new accounts, privilege changes, or configuration changes on the firewall/VPN?
  • What monitoring/alerting is in place for suspicious VPN logins and admin changes, and who gets alerted?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

CISA has issued advice urging organisations to harden Fortinet devices following the reported credential exposure. Separately, CISA has also warned of active exploitation affecting Splunk Enterprise in some environments; if your IT provider runs Splunk, confirm they are treating this as urgent.

One action today

Today, confirm whether your business uses Fortinet SSL VPN/FortiGate on the internet and, if yes, have your IT provider terminate active sessions and reset VPN/admin passwords while verifying MFA is enforced for all accounts.
