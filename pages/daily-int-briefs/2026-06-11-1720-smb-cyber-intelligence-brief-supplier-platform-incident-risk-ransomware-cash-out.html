SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief: Supplier platform incident risk + ransomware cash-out disruption

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Thursday 11 June 2026, 17:20 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier/SaaS incident scams and follow-on phishing (ServiceNow-themed)

What to look out for today

Supplier/SaaS incident follow-ons: a reported incident where a flaw was exploited to gain deeper unauthorised access to some ServiceNow customer instances. Expect knock-on scams that impersonate IT service desks, “ServiceNow support”, or your own MSP to capture logins or approve “urgent security changes”.

Ransomware disruption themes: authorities dismantling a crypto-laundering service allegedly used by ransomware groups can trigger criminals to pivot tactics (new payment routes, new scams, more pressure on victims).

Data breach ripple effects: a major regulatory fine tied to a large breach is a reminder that regulators and customers expect strong controls, documentation, and rapid response when personal data is involved.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • ServiceNow is embedded in day-to-day operations for many SMEs via ticketing, IT workflows, HR/service portals, and supplier support. If your supplier uses it, you can still be targeted through email and phone social engineering.
  • Incident-driven phishing is highly effective: attackers copy real vendor language and time their messages to real news, increasing the chance staff will click or comply.
  • Ransomware groups adapt quickly. Even if a laundering route is disrupted, attempted break-ins, extortion emails and fake “negotiator” approaches may increase.

Warning signs

  • Emails or calls claiming your ServiceNow instance (or your supplier’s) is “affected” and you must reset passwords, approve a security update, or add an admin user urgently.
  • Requests to share one-time passcodes, MFA prompts, recovery codes, or to “verify” credentials to keep services running.
  • Unexpected support tickets created on your behalf, or notifications about workflow changes you didn’t request.
  • Any “incident response” invoice or retainer request that arrives out of the blue, especially with new bank details.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation scams: pretending to be ServiceNow, your MSP, or a supplier’s IT team to harvest logins or get staff to approve access.
  • Helpdesk/workflow abuse: using tickets, portal messages, or “case updates” to drive clicks and credential entry.
  • MFA fatigue / approval prompts: pushing repeated sign-in prompts then calling to “help you stop them”.
  • Extortion and fear tactics: leveraging ransomware news to pressure businesses into rushed payments or “security purchases”.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (10 minutes): no one should act on vendor/security incident emails or calls without using a known-good contact method (internal directory / contract paperwork), not the details provided in the message.
  • Lock down support channels: ensure your helpdesk/portal processes require verification before password resets, new admin accounts, or bank-detail changes.
  • Review admin access: confirm who has admin rights in key SaaS platforms (including ticketing/ITSM) and remove stale accounts.
  • Prepare for disruption: confirm you can operate if your ticketing/portal is degraded—how do staff raise incidents, and who approves emergency access?

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we use ServiceNow directly, or do any key suppliers use it for our support? If yes, what’s our verification process for incident-related requests?
  • What monitoring is in place for new admin accounts, unusual logins, or suspicious workflow changes in our SaaS tools?
  • If we get a vendor-impersonation email, what is the one internal place we should forward it to, and what’s the expected response time?
  • For ransomware readiness: what’s our restore test date for critical systems and Microsoft 365/Google Workspace data?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Service providers may apply security updates quickly during incidents. If you run or rely on ServiceNow (or a managed service that uses it), ask your provider to confirm whether any action is required on your side (for example, credential resets or configuration checks) and how they’re validating that customer access remains appropriate.

One action today

Send a short internal note: “Treat any ServiceNow/MSP ‘urgent security’ email or call as suspicious—verify via known contact details before logging in, approving access, or sharing MFA codes.”
