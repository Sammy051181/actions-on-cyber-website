SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief — phishing breaches, macOS infostealers, and perimeter credential harvesting

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 24 June 2026, 11:28 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing-led account compromise and credential theft (including Mac users) plus perimeter device credential harvesting

What to look out for today

Today’s theme is credential theft: phishing that leads to account takeover, plus campaigns targeting macOS users and attempts to harvest or reuse credentials from internet-facing network devices. Expect more convincing “urgent” messages designed to push staff into quick actions.

  • Phishing that leads to real data exposure (not just nuisance spam).
  • macOS “fix” / ClickFix-style prompts that nudge users to run actions that install an information-stealing malware.
  • Firewall/perimeter credential harvesting that can lead to remote access and follow-on ransomware/extortion.
  • Supplier incident noise (attack news triggers copycat scams: fake invoices, fake “security resets”, fake breach notifications).

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs often rely on email, cloud logins and outsourced IT. If attackers steal credentials, they can:

  • Access Microsoft 365/Google accounts and quietly read invoices, payment instructions and payroll emails.
  • Use stolen browser passwords/session data from an infected Mac/PC to log into banking, accounting, CRM and file-sharing tools.
  • Break in via a managed firewall/VPN and disrupt operations or deploy ransomware.

Warning signs

  • Emails or chat messages urging a “quick fix” or “security update” and telling staff to follow unusual steps (especially on Macs).
  • Unexpected MFA prompts (“Approve sign-in”) or password reset emails staff didn’t request.
  • New inbox rules/forwarding set up in finance or leadership mailboxes.
  • Sudden login alerts from new locations/devices, especially for admin, finance, payroll or shared accounts.
  • Clients/suppliers receiving strange messages “from you” asking to confirm payments or share files.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Phishing-to-internal access: a single successful phish can give access to email and internal systems, as seen in a reported healthtech breach attributed to phishing.
  • Mac-focused credential theft: “Click here to fix” style lures can lead to infostealer installation, which then steals saved passwords and session tokens.
  • Perimeter credential harvesting: large-scale collection of firewall/VPN credentials increases the chance that reused passwords or weak authentication will be exploited—particularly impacting MSP-managed estates.
  • Incident-themed scams: public cyberattack/breach headlines are commonly reused by criminals to send fake “we’ve been breached” or “re-validate your account” messages.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): no one should follow emailed “fix steps” or run commands. Any “security update” must go via IT/helpdesk.
  • Lock down finance workflows: require a call-back to a known number for any bank detail changes, urgent payments, or new payees.
  • Check email account hygiene: review mailbox forwarding rules and suspicious sign-ins for finance/admin users.
  • Confirm MFA coverage: ensure MFA is enabled on email, remote access/VPN, accounting, payroll, and admin consoles.
  • Mac controls: ensure staff can’t install unapproved software; remind users not to open unexpected disk image files or installers.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have recent sign-in monitoring and alerts for impossible travel, new devices, and suspicious inbox rules?
  • Which users are highest risk (finance/admin) and do they have strong MFA and conditional access controls?
  • Are our firewall/VPN/admin interfaces exposed to the internet, and what extra controls are in place (MFA, IP allow-listing, admin access restrictions)?
  • Do we enforce unique passwords for device/admin accounts and rotate them if supplier reports suggest credential harvesting in the wild?
  • How quickly can we isolate a device and invalidate sessions if an infostealer is suspected?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

There are reports of active exploitation against a Cisco Unified Communications Manager flaw (CVE-2026-20230). If you use Cisco call/voice infrastructure (especially internet-reachable or externally managed), ask your IT/provider to confirm whether you are affected and what mitigations/updates are in place—this is more about preventing disruption and unauthorised access than chasing technical detail.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “Do not follow emailed ‘fix’ instructions or install unexpected Mac disk images; report immediately and wait for IT confirmation.”
