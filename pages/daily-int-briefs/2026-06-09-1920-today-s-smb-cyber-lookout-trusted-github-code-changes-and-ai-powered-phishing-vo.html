Today’s SMB cyber lookout: “trusted” GitHub code changes and AI-powered phishing volume

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Tuesday 09 June 2026, 19:20 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier ecosystem risk (GitHub/Microsoft repos) + phishing that looks more convincing

What to look out for today

1) Supplier ecosystem incident: Microsoft had multiple GitHub repositories disabled (and some later restored) after a security incident where projects were compromised to inject password-stealing malware into code. This can cause disruption (broken CI/CD) and increases the chance of unsafe dependencies or copied snippets entering business systems.

2) Phishing volume and quality jump: Reporting continues to highlight how AI is making phishing faster to produce and harder to spot, increasing the volume of realistic-looking messages that can slip past busy teams.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Ripple effects: Even if you’re not a Microsoft customer, you may rely on open-source projects, sample code, GitHub Actions, or templates that reference Microsoft repos.
  • Operational disruption: If your developer/website supplier or MSP pulls code automatically, a repo being taken offline can break builds and deployments at short notice.
  • Increased scam opportunity: Attackers commonly piggyback on big news with emails like “security update required” or “reconnect your GitHub/Microsoft account”, aiming to steal credentials.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected build/deployment failures mentioning missing/removed GitHub repositories or unavailable dependencies.
  • New or changed third-party packages, actions, or “helper scripts” appearing in projects without a clear business reason.
  • Emails/DMs claiming to be from GitHub/Microsoft asking you to re-authenticate, reset tokens, or approve a new app urgently.
  • Staff receiving unusually well-written, role-specific emails (finance, HR, IT) that still try to push a link or attachment quickly.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Supply-chain infection: Compromise a trusted repo (or something that looks like it), then wait for automated pulls into real environments.
  • Impersonation scams: Use the news to send believable “incident response” messages that capture GitHub/Microsoft credentials or session tokens.
  • Credential theft follow-on: Stolen passwords can be used to access email, file storage, payroll, customer data, and payment workflows.

What to do today

  • Tell staff what’s happening: A short internal note: “Expect fake GitHub/Microsoft security emails. Don’t log in via email links.”
  • Check your dependencies and automation: If you (or your supplier) use GitHub Actions, templates, or auto-updating dependencies, confirm you have a review/approval step for changes that affect production.
  • Lock down access: Ensure MFA is enabled for GitHub and Microsoft accounts used for work, and review who has admin rights and long-lived tokens.
  • Prepare for disruption: If your website/app deploys from GitHub, confirm you can pause deployments safely and roll back if something looks off.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have an inventory of where we pull code/templates/actions from (GitHub repos, packages, third-party scripts)?
  • Do we require approvals for dependency/action changes before they can reach production?
  • How do we detect suspicious changes in build pipelines (unexpected new steps, new external downloads, new secrets usage)?
  • Are MFA and least-privilege enforced for GitHub/Microsoft admin accounts and service accounts?
  • If a key repo or dependency is suddenly removed/disabled, what’s our continuity plan for deployments?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your organisation runs operational technology or building/energy management equipment (e.g., inverters, industrial gateways or managed switches), note that new ICS advisories were published today. These are unlikely to affect most office-only SMEs, but schools, facilities, manufacturers and charities with estates should ask their supplier/maintainer to confirm whether any Schneider Electric or Siemens KACO devices are in use and whether mitigations/updates are planned.

One action today

Send a same-day staff alert: ‘Ignore GitHub/Microsoft “security incident” login/reset emails—only sign in via bookmarks, and report anything asking you to approve a new app or re-authenticate.’
