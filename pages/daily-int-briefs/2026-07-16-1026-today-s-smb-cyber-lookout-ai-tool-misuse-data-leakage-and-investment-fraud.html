Today’s SMB cyber lookout: AI tool misuse, data leakage, and investment fraud

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Thursday 16 July 2026, 10:26 UK time
Today’s look-out: AI-assisted scams and unapproved AI tools creating data leakage and account risk

What to look out for today

Three themes to brief staff on today:

  • Unapproved AI tools and browser add-ons being used in day-to-day work (including generative AI websites, AI assistants, and browser extensions) that may see sensitive information copied into them.
  • AI tools being abused by criminals to support hacking activity and run malicious operations, increasing the pace and scale of attacks.
  • Investment fraud approaches (get-rich-quick, crypto, “guaranteed returns”) continuing at scale, with criminals targeting individuals and sometimes finance teams.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs often rely heavily on SaaS, browsers, and outsourced IT, which means a lot of business data now flows through web apps and add-ons. If staff paste customer data, payroll details, contracts, pricing, or internal documents into AI tools (or install “helpful” extensions), you can create a quiet data leak even without a traditional breach. Separately, as criminals adopt AI-enabled tooling, phishing and account takeover attempts can become more convincing and more frequent.

Warning signs

  • Staff installing new browser extensions or “AI assistants” to speed up admin work, meeting notes, sales emails, or document drafting.
  • People copying/pasting sensitive material (client lists, invoices, contracts, health/HR info) into public AI chat tools to “summarise” or “rewrite”.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts, password reset emails, or repeated sign-in alerts for Microsoft 365/Google/other SaaS.
  • Messages promoting investments with urgency, secrecy, or pressure to move funds quickly.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Data harvesting via workflow habits: attackers don’t always need to hack you if staff voluntarily feed sensitive content into the wrong tool or extension.
  • Faster, more tailored phishing: AI-assisted writing can produce more believable lures aimed at payroll, finance, and executives.
  • Abuse of legitimate AI tooling: criminals may use widely available AI utilities as “helpers” in malicious campaigns, making low-skill attacks easier to run.

What to do today

  • Set a simple rule for AI use: what staff can/can’t paste into AI tools (e.g. “no personal data, no client confidentials, no credentials, no bank details, no contracts”).
  • Review browser extensions: remove anything unused, unknown, or not business-approved—especially “AI” or “productivity” add-ons.
  • Re-brief finance staff: treat investment approaches and “urgent opportunity” messages as suspicious; keep business banking steps segregated and approved.
  • Reinforce account protection: ensure MFA is enabled for key SaaS (email, file storage, accounting, payroll) and that staff report unexpected MFA prompts immediately.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have a policy and technical controls for approved vs unapproved AI tools (and do we log or restrict risky ones)?
  • Can we manage and restrict browser extensions on company devices?
  • What’s our current coverage for SaaS account takeover detection (impossible travel alerts, risky sign-ins, MFA fatigue patterns)?
  • How do we quickly disable access and reset sessions if a staff account is suspected compromised?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No broad patch-driven action for most SMEs from today’s items. If you run specialist industrial/edge gateway equipment (e.g., ABB Ability Edgenius), confirm with your supplier/IT provider whether it’s in your environment and who owns updates for it.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “Don’t paste sensitive business or personal data into AI tools, and don’t install AI browser extensions without approval—report any unexpected MFA prompts immediately.”
