Today’s SMB cyber lookout: service-desk scams, ransomware footholds and network device risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 24 June 2026, 16:07 UK time
Today’s look-out: Service-desk social engineering and ransomware access attempts (plus internet-facing network device exposure)

What to look out for today

  • Service desk social engineering: attackers phoning/emailing to trigger password resets, MFA changes, or “urgent” account recovery.
  • Ransomware access-broker activity: stealthy backdoor activity linked to financially motivated actors targeting sectors including education, IT and professional services.
  • Internet-facing network equipment risk: warnings that certain widely used devices are being actively exploited in the wild.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Smaller organisations often rely on a lean IT function (or an outsourced provider) and a busy front desk/service desk. That makes “helpful” staff and quick processes a prime target. If an attacker can persuade someone to reset a password or change MFA, they may gain access to email, cloud files, payroll, payments, and customer data. From there, disruption and ransomware can follow quickly.

Warning signs

  • Calls claiming to be a senior colleague, IT provider, or supplier asking for a password reset or MFA reset.
  • Pressure tactics: “I’m locked out”, “board meeting in 10 minutes”, “this is a security emergency”.
  • Requests to change the phone number/email used for MFA or account recovery.
  • Unusual login prompts or “MFA reset” notifications that nobody requested.
  • Unexpected remote-support requests or insistence on using a new support channel.
  • New admin accounts, odd mailbox rules, or unexplained forwarding in Microsoft 365/Google Workspace.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Impersonation of staff to convince a service desk (internal or outsourced) to reset credentials or bypass MFA.
  • Account takeover of email to run invoice fraud (payment diversion), supplier impersonation, and internal phishing.
  • Ransomware “foothold then expand”: once inside, attackers may quietly establish persistence and move towards business disruption.
  • Exposed edge devices (e.g., commonly deployed networking/management gear) can be targeted to gain a stepping stone into the network.

What to do today

  • Set a strict reset process: no MFA resets or password resets from inbound requests unless identity is verified via a separate, known-good channel.
  • Add a “high-risk change” rule: changing MFA factors, recovery email/phone, or admin rights requires manager approval (or a second person) and a logged ticket.
  • Brief reception/service desk staff (including your MSP’s helpdesk): share two examples of scripts scammers use and the exact steps to refuse and escalate.
  • Review admin access: ensure admin accounts are separate from day-to-day accounts and protected with strong MFA.
  • Check email controls: monitor for new forwarding rules and suspicious mailbox permissions.
  • Backups & recovery check: confirm you can restore key systems and that backups are protected from deletion by compromised accounts.

Ask your IT provider

  • What is our documented process for password resets and MFA resets, and how do you verify identity for urgent requests?
  • Do you enforce two-person approval for MFA factor changes, recovery detail changes, and new admin creation?
  • Are we monitoring for new mailbox forwarding rules, suspicious sign-ins, and privilege changes in our cloud tenant?
  • Which of our internet-facing devices (firewalls, Wi‑Fi controllers, remote management) are exposed, and how quickly do you respond to “actively exploited” warnings?
  • If ransomware hits, what is the restore plan and realistic time to recover email, files, finance and line-of-business apps?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

CISA has flagged active exploitation affecting certain network equipment (including UniFi OS and serial-to-ethernet devices). If you use managed networking or remote access equipment, ask your IT provider to confirm whether any of your internet-facing devices are in scope and what immediate mitigations or updates have been applied.

One action today

Send a same-day note to anyone who can reset passwords/MFA (including your MSP helpdesk) to enforce “no MFA reset or recovery detail change without call-back on a known number + manager approval”.
