Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

AI-powered phishing-as-a-service disrupted — expect copycat login and payment phishing

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Sunday 14 June 2026, 18:47 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing themes (logins and card payments) and knock-on risk for security tooling

What to look out for today

Expect an uptick in convincing, mass-produced phishing that targets:

  • Business email and cloud logins (Microsoft 365/Google-style sign-in pages)
  • Card payment details (requests to “verify” or “re-enter” card details)
  • Password resets and MFA prompts designed to trick staff into approving a login

This follows news that law enforcement and major providers have disrupted a large-scale phishing-as-a-service operation used to steal credentials and card data. When big services are taken down, criminals often rebuild quickly and shift to new domains and brands.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Phishing remains the quickest route to invoice fraud, payroll diversion, and account takeover.
  • Even if the takedown is successful, copycat campaigns and “cleanup” scams (“we detected fraud on your account”) often follow.
  • Stolen credentials can be reused to access cloud email, file storage, and bookkeeping tools, leading to payment redirection and data exposure.

Warning signs

  • Emails or texts claiming your account is locked, a payment failed, or a security alert needs “immediate” action.
  • Links that go to unfamiliar domains, or login pages that appear slightly “off” (odd wording, unusual branding, unexpected extra fields).
  • Requests to re-enter card details or “confirm” banking details to avoid service interruption.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts or password reset emails that nobody requested.
  • Staff reporting that a link “looked normal” but led to a login page they weren’t expecting.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • High-volume, AI-assisted phishing to personalise lures (job titles, supplier names, roles like Finance/HR) at scale.
  • Credential harvesting for Microsoft 365/Google accounts, then using the mailbox to send internal-looking payment change requests.
  • Card theft by impersonating common services (delivery, subscription renewals, software billing) and pushing “verify your payment”.
  • Follow-on social engineering: once an attacker has a foothold, they may call pretending to be “IT support” to bypass controls.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff warning (especially to Finance and anyone who approves payments): do not enter passwords or card details from emailed links; go via bookmarks or known apps.
  • Reinforce payment-change process: any supplier bank detail change must be verified via a known phone number (not the email thread).
  • Check MFA hygiene: remind staff to reject unexpected MFA prompts and report them immediately.
  • Review mailbox forwarding rules and “new inbox rules” alerts if your provider supports it (a common sign of takeover).
  • Make reporting easy: one place to forward suspicious messages (or a “report phishing” button) and a clear internal contact.

Ask your IT provider

  • Are we enforcing MFA on email and key SaaS tools, and do we have conditional access (or equivalent) to block risky sign-ins?
  • Do we have alerts for new inbox rules/forwarding and suspicious sign-in behaviour?
  • Can we tighten controls for Finance (e.g. stronger MFA, restricted login locations, additional monitoring)?
  • Do we have a tested process to quickly reset compromised accounts and revoke sessions if a user enters credentials?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If you (or your MSP) run Splunk Enterprise internally, ask whether you’re affected by the latest Splunk Enterprise security update for a critical issue (CVE-2026-20253). This is mainly relevant to organisations operating Splunk on-prem; if you don’t use Splunk, you can ignore this.

One action today

Send a short internal note today: “Don’t log in or enter card details via email links; use bookmarks/apps. Reject unexpected MFA prompts and report them immediately.”
