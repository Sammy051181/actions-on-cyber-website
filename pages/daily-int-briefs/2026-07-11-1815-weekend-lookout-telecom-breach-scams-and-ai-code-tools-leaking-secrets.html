Weekend lookout: telecom-breach scams and AI code tools leaking secrets

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

Moderate Saturday 11 July 2026, 18:15 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier incident scams + AI tooling secrets exposure

What to look out for today

Two themes to brief your team on this weekend:

  • Telecom breach ripple effects: scams that reference a real-world telecom/security incident to sound believable (e.g., “urgent SIM swap”, “account re-verification”, “new direct debit”).
  • AI coding/review tools handling secrets unsafely: prompt-injection hidden in images that can trick AI agents into exposing repository secrets (API keys, tokens, .env values) into code, tickets, or logs.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • SMEs are easier to pressure: if attackers can convincingly reference a telecom breach, they can push staff into rushed actions (password resets, MFA changes, sending OTP codes, changing payment details).
  • AI tools can create a new “leak path”: even if your repo permissions are right, an AI agent that reads files can be manipulated into copying secrets into places they don’t belong (pull requests, comments, generated code), creating a downstream breach.
  • Operational disruption: account takeovers (email/telecom) often lead to invoice fraud, payroll diversion, and lockouts from key SaaS services.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected emails/texts/calls claiming to be from a mobile or broadband provider about a “security incident”, asking you to verify identity, share a one-time code, or click a link.
  • Supplier/payment change requests that reference a “provider breach” as the reason for new bank details or a “temporary workaround”.
  • In your dev workflow: pull requests or tool output that suddenly includes long numeric strings, “encoded” blobs, or odd lists that weren’t requested.
  • AI assistants or code review bots asking for broader access (“to be helpful”) such as reading .env files, secrets stores, or CI variables.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Breach-themed social engineering: criminals reuse news of a real incident to add urgency and credibility, aiming to capture logins/OTPs or to redirect payments.
  • Account takeover chain: compromise of telecom-related access can enable password resets for email and SaaS accounts, leading to invoice fraud and data theft.
  • AI prompt injection via images: a malicious image (e.g., in a repo, issue, or attachment) can contain hidden instructions that an AI agent follows, potentially causing it to disclose secrets into code or collaboration tools.

What to do today

  • Finance & ops: re-issue your “no change of bank details without call-back” rule. Call back using a known number from your records, not one in the email.
  • All staff: remind people: never share one-time passcodes (OTP) and don’t “confirm” MFA prompts you didn’t initiate.
  • IT/admin: ensure key accounts (email, payroll, banking, accounting) have MFA enabled and that recovery options aren’t tied only to a single phone number.
  • Dev/agency teams using AI tools: keep secrets out of repos; reduce AI tool permissions; block/strip secrets from logs; and add a quick check so PRs don’t introduce credentials or unusual encoded content.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have a documented process for handling supplier “bank detail change” requests and do we test it?
  • Can you confirm MFA and recovery settings for Microsoft 365/Google, payroll, and finance platforms are in place and monitored?
  • Do we have alerting for suspicious email rule changes/forwarding and unusual sign-ins?
  • If we use AI coding/review tools, what data do they have access to (repos, tickets, chat), and how are secrets prevented from being exposed in outputs?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your business uses embedded devices (routers, cameras, specialist appliances) supplied/managed by a third party, ask the supplier or MSP how they track and update firmware components such as bootloaders. Treat “we don’t know” as a risk signal and request a plan for regular firmware maintenance and device lifecycle replacement.

One action today

Send a short internal note today: “No one shares OTP/MFA codes, and any bank-detail change must be verified by call-back to a known number.”
