Today’s SMB cyber lookout: software supply-chain tampering + Canvas breach fallout scams

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Tuesday 12 May 2026, 14:27 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier and software supply-chain risk (developer tooling) + supplier-incident themed phishing

What to look out for today

Two themes to watch closely:

  • Software supply-chain tampering affecting developer ecosystems (npm/PyPI packages) and a Jenkins Marketplace plugin. This matters if you build software, run CI/CD pipelines, or use an MSP/dev partner that does.
  • Supplier-incident themed scams riding on the news of a major education technology breach involving Instructure/Canvas (schools, colleges, training providers and anyone using related services should be alert for impersonation emails).

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Indirect impact is common: you might not use the affected tools directly, but your web agency, software supplier, or MSP might. Compromised build tools can lead to compromised websites, apps, or updates delivered to customers.
  • Business disruption risk: if credentials are stolen from developers or CI systems, attackers can move into cloud accounts, source code, customer data, and even payment or email systems.
  • Scam risk spikes after big incidents: when a supplier breach hits the headlines, criminals often send convincing “security update”, “password reset”, “invoice re-issue”, or “new bank details” messages.

Warning signs

  • Emails claiming to be from Canvas/Instructure (or your LMS provider) asking you to re-authenticate, download a “security tool”, or urgently reset passwords via a link.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts for email, Microsoft 365/Google Workspace, GitHub/GitLab, cloud consoles, or finance platforms.
  • Your IT/dev team reports unusual behaviour in build pipelines: new dependencies, odd package updates, or unexpected outbound connections.
  • Jenkins admins notice a plugin update they didn’t schedule, or new credentials/secrets accessed unexpectedly.
  • Suppliers requesting payment detail changes “due to the breach” or asking to re-issue invoices.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Trojanised packages/plugins can steal browser/session tokens, API keys and passwords from developer machines and CI systems.
  • Stolen access can be used to change code (e.g., inserting skimmers, backdoors, or redirecting payments) and then ship it as a legitimate update.
  • Attackers may leverage the Canvas/Instructure news to run high-volume phishing against schools, charities, and training providers—especially targeting admin accounts and helpdesks.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (especially finance, IT, and school admin teams): treat any supplier “security notice” or “bank change” as suspicious; verify via known phone numbers.
  • Check who builds/maintains your website or apps (internal team or supplier). Ask if they use npm/PyPI heavily, and whether they’ve checked for suspicious dependency updates.
  • If you run Jenkins (or your MSP does): confirm plugin changes are controlled, and investigate any unexpected plugin updates or new credentials access.
  • Lock down key accounts: ensure MFA is enabled for email, cloud, accounting, and code repositories; review recent sign-ins for anomalies.
  • Prepare for customer queries: if you’re a school/training provider using Canvas, decide who handles parent/student/vendor calls and what you will say if asked about data exposure.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do any of our suppliers (web/dev/MSP) use Jenkins, npm, or PyPI in production build pipelines? If yes, what checks have been done since these reports?
  • How do you control software updates to build tools and plugins (approval process, logging, and rollback plan)?
  • Do we have alerting for suspicious logins and MFA fatigue (repeated prompts) on Microsoft 365/Google, cloud consoles, and admin portals?
  • If a supplier is breached, what is our playbook for verifying requests, communicating internally, and protecting payments?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

SAP has published its May 2026 security updates. If you use SAP Commerce Cloud or S/4HANA, make sure your SAP support partner has a clear plan and timeline to apply relevant fixes and confirm any additional monitoring during the change window.

One action today

Send a 5-minute internal alert: “No supplier payment changes or ‘security resets’ are actioned from email alone—verify via a known phone number,” and remind staff to report unexpected MFA prompts immediately.
