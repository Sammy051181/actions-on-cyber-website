Browser add-ons and “fix-it” lures: practical checks to reduce ransomware risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Thursday 25 June 2026, 11:20 UK time
Today’s look-out: Malicious browser extensions, “click-to-fix” style lures, and network edge device compromise

What to look out for today

Today’s themes are about initial access: attackers getting a foothold through everyday tools. Watch for:

  • Malicious Microsoft Edge extensions used as a stepping-stone from the browser into the PC, then on to ransomware-style activity.
  • “ClickFix” / “do this quick fix” style prompts that lead users into installing or running something they shouldn’t, followed by stealthy backdoor behaviour.
  • Supplier/edge risk: if your organisation (or your MSP) runs Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN, recent reporting highlights real-world attacks against it.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs often standardise on a browser + extensions + SaaS tools, and rely on outsourced IT for network edge devices. That combination means one unsafe extension, one rushed “fix this” prompt, or one compromised edge appliance can quickly turn into:

  • credential theft and account takeover (email, Microsoft 365/Google, payroll, banking portals);
  • silent remote access (backdoors) that bypasses “normal” antivirus expectations;
  • ransomware disruption and business downtime.

Warning signs

  • Staff report a new browser extension they don’t remember installing, or an extension asking for broad permissions (e.g., “read and change all data on websites”).
  • Unusual browser pop-ups telling users to “verify”, “fix”, “update”, or “run a quick command” to continue.
  • Unexpected security prompts on PCs (new admin/password prompts) or sudden new background processes reported by your IT provider.
  • New user accounts, configuration changes, or strange admin activity reported on your network edge device/portal by your MSP.
  • Users locked out of accounts, or MFA prompts appearing when nobody is logging in.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Extensions as a bridge: a malicious Edge add-on can be used to move from the browser into the underlying computer, then drop further tooling and maintain access.
  • Social engineering “fix” lures: users are pressured to take a “quick step” to restore access to a document, website, invoice, or shared file—leading to malware installation and a backdoor.
  • Edge device compromise: if an organisation’s SD-WAN is attacked, criminals can gain deep access and pivot into internal systems, increasing ransomware risk.

What to do today

  • Audit browser extensions (Edge/Chrome): remove anything not needed for work; confirm the owner/publisher and why it’s required.
  • Lock down extension installation for staff who don’t need it (use allow-lists where possible) and stop “random add-ons” becoming normal.
  • Run a quick phishing reminder: “No ‘fix your account’ steps from pop-ups. If in doubt, report it.”
  • Check logging/alerts for unusual sign-ins and MFA fatigue. Make sure alerts go to a monitored mailbox/phone.
  • Backups and restore check: confirm you can restore one key business system or file set quickly (a simple test beats assumptions).

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you provide a list of approved browser extensions for our business, and block everything else?
  • What is our process for investigating a report like “I clicked a fix prompt” or “an extension appeared” — and how fast can we isolate a device?
  • Do we (or you) operate Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN anywhere in our environment? If yes, what extra monitoring is in place for suspicious admin activity and configuration changes?
  • Are our endpoint protections and logs set up to catch persistence/backdoor behaviour, not just obvious malware files?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If you or your MSP uses Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN, treat this as a priority supplier/edge review: confirm your provider has checked for signs of compromise and has applied vendor guidance promptly. Even when a fix exists, the key SME question is whether you have monitoring and change control that would spot rogue admin activity quickly.

One action today

Do a 15-minute audit of all Edge/Chrome extensions on company devices today and remove (or centrally block) anything not explicitly approved for work.
