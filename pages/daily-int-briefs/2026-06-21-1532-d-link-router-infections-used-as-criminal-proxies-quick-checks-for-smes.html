Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

D-Link router infections used as criminal proxies: quick checks for SMEs

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Sunday 21 June 2026, 15:32 UK time
Today’s look-out: Compromised routers and “proxy” abuse leading to account takeovers and fraud

What to look out for today

Reports say a botnet (“AryStinger”) has compromised thousands of older D-Link routers and is using them as proxies for criminal traffic. If your office or remote sites still use an ageing router, it could be at higher risk of being hijacked and used in the background.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Business disruption: an infected router can cause slow, unstable internet, VPN issues, and intermittent access to cloud apps.
  • Security blind spots: criminals can route traffic through hijacked routers, making suspicious activity look like it’s coming from “normal” UK business connections.
  • Knock-on risk: if your IP address is used for malicious activity, you can see account lockouts, extra verification challenges, or blocks from SaaS providers.
  • Remote working risk: home-office routers used by staff can be a weak link into business systems (especially where VPN or remote access is used).

Warning signs

  • Internet connection suddenly slower or unstable without an ISP fault reported.
  • Staff report frequent re-logins, MFA prompts, or “unusual activity” flags on business accounts.
  • New/unknown devices showing in your router admin page, or router settings changed (DNS, admin account, remote management enabled).
  • Unexpected outbound traffic alerts from your firewall/IT provider, or your ISP contacts you about suspicious traffic.
  • Services you use (email, accounting, cloud storage) start rate-limiting or blocking logins from your office IP address.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Using hijacked routers as stepping stones (proxies) to hide the real source of phishing, credential stuffing, and other attacks.
  • Targeting organisations that still rely on default passwords, old routers, or exposed remote administration.
  • Using compromised routers to support fraud attempts (e.g., logins to business email or finance systems that appear to come from a “legitimate” business IP address).

What to do today

  • Identify what router you’re using at each site (office, warehouse, shop, and any remote locations). Note the model and approx. age.
  • Change router admin passwords (unique, strong) and confirm remote administration is disabled unless your IT provider explicitly requires it and has locked it down.
  • Check DNS settings in the router admin page; if they are unfamiliar, treat as a red flag and escalate to your IT support.
  • Ask for a quick review of outbound traffic (do you have unusual spikes, especially to unfamiliar destinations?).
  • If the router is old/out of support, plan a replacement with business-grade kit (and keep a record of who manages it: ISP, MSP, or in-house).

Ask your IT provider

  • Which routers are in use across our sites, and which are end-of-life or no longer receiving updates?
  • Do we have remote management enabled on any router? If yes, how is it restricted (IP allowlist, MFA, VPN-only access)?
  • How do you detect if our connection is being used as a proxy for malicious traffic (alerts, thresholds, ISP liaison)?
  • If our public IP gets blocked or reputation-damaged, what’s the recovery plan (ISP ticketing, IP change, evidence for SaaS providers)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

This story is mainly about older routers. If your router is no longer supported, updates may not be available and replacement is often the safest route. If it is supported, ensure the router firmware is managed and updated as part of your normal IT maintenance.

One action today

Make a quick list of every router used by your business (including remote sites) and confirm none are end-of-life or using default/unknown admin credentials.
