Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: fake Microsoft 365 “passkey enrolment” calls

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 10 July 2026, 15:50 UK time
Today’s look-out: Identity phishing via fake security calls and passkey enrolment

What to look out for today

Watch for phone calls (and follow-up messages) pretending to be Microsoft, your IT provider, or “security”, urging staff to enrol a new Microsoft Entra passkey or approve a “security change” to keep their account safe. The aim is to get access to Microsoft 365 and then move to data theft and extortion.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

If attackers get into one Microsoft 365 mailbox or OneDrive/SharePoint account, they can quickly:

  • download sensitive files (client data, HR, finance, contracts);
  • read emails for payment and invoice fraud opportunities;
  • use internal email threads to convincingly scam customers/suppliers;
  • attempt further takeovers (other users, shared mailboxes, admin accounts).

SMEs are particularly exposed because busy teams often treat “security verification” requests as urgent, and IT support may be outsourced (making “we’re calling from IT” sound plausible).

Warning signs

  • A call claiming there is an urgent account risk and you must “enrol a passkey now”.
  • Pressure to act immediately, secrecy (“don’t raise a ticket, we’re handling it”).
  • Requests to read out codes, approve prompts, or follow a link to “set up passkey / security key”.
  • Unexpected Microsoft 365 sign-in or security notifications you didn’t initiate.
  • New sign-in prompts occurring while you are on the phone with the caller.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Social engineering over the phone to build trust and urgency (“we detected suspicious access”).
  • Steering the victim into adding a new authentication method (e.g., passkey enrolment) that benefits the attacker.
  • Microsoft 365 access used for rapid data collection, then extortion threats.
  • Mailbox reconnaissance to identify invoices, payroll, or supplier payments to target next.

What to do today

  • Send a short staff alert: IT/Microsoft will not call asking you to enrol a passkey or approve sign-in prompts on demand. If in doubt, hang up and call back via a known number.
  • Reinforce a “stop and verify” process for any security request received by phone, especially for Microsoft 365 accounts.
  • Review Microsoft 365 sign-in alerts and check for unexpected changes to authentication methods (especially for senior staff, finance, and admins).
  • Make sure leavers/contractor access is tight and that shared mailboxes are monitored (they’re often overlooked).
  • Prepare an internal response step: if someone thinks they complied, they should report immediately (no blame) so access can be cut quickly.

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you audit recent changes to authentication methods (including passkeys) in our Microsoft Entra/Microsoft 365 tenant?
  • Do we have alerting for new authentication method enrolment and risky sign-ins for key users (admins/finance/directors)?
  • Which accounts have elevated privileges, and do they have stronger sign-in controls than standard users?
  • If a user reports a suspected enrolment or prompt approval, what is the rapid containment playbook (account lock, session revoke, password reset, mailbox rules check, file access review)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No specific patch action from today’s main story. However, take this as a prompt to keep Microsoft 365 identity and access settings under regular review, because many modern compromises succeed through deception and account takeovers rather than unpatched PCs.

One action today

Send a same-day staff message: “If anyone calls claiming to be Microsoft/IT and asks you to enrol a passkey or approve sign-in prompts, hang up and report it—do not proceed.”
