Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: callback phishing via order tracking + ZIP ‘photo’ lures + SIM-swap account takeovers

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 26 June 2026, 11:28 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing and account takeover via trusted apps and phone-based social engineering

What to look out for today

Watch for a rise in “callback phishing” and fake order/receipt activity that pushes staff to call a phone number, hand over details, or install remote support tools. Also watch for ZIP attachments themed as photos/documents (notably affecting hospitality/front-desk style roles), and SIM-swap driven account takeovers where criminals intercept SMS codes.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

These attacks don’t rely on complex hacking. They target normal operations: deliveries, customer bookings, invoices, and password resets. If a criminal gets remote access to a PC or takes over an email account, it can quickly lead to payment diversion, payroll fraud, data theft, and business disruption.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected “receipt”, “refund”, or “purchase confirmation” appearing in an order-tracking app or related notifications, especially if no-one in the business made the purchase.
  • Messages that urge you to call a number to “stop a charge”, “confirm delivery”, or “resolve an account issue”.
  • Pressure to install remote access / remote support software (or “screen sharing”) to “fix” a delivery, payment, or account problem.
  • Emails with ZIP attachments labelled as photos, booking images, or customer documents—especially if sent to reception/front desk/shared inboxes.
  • Sudden loss of mobile signal, SIM going to “No Service”, or unexpected operator messages—often a sign of SIM-swap attempts.
  • Staff reporting they can’t log in because MFA codes stop arriving or password reset notifications appear they didn’t request.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Callback phishing: criminals seed fake order activity to trigger worry and get staff to phone them, then they social engineer for passwords, card details, or persuade installation of remote access tools.
  • ZIP lures: a “photo” or “booking” ZIP is used to trick someone into opening content that installs malware, often on high-availability machines (e.g., front desk PCs).
  • SIM-swap: criminals hijack a phone number so they can intercept SMS-based login/reset codes and take over email, Microsoft 365/Google accounts, banking, or payment services.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): “No calling numbers from unexpected receipts/refunds/delivery issues; no installing remote support tools from unsolicited messages.”
  • Change the habit: if something looks wrong, staff should use known contact routes (supplier website/app accessed directly, known phone numbers already on file—not those provided in the message).
  • Block or quarantine ZIP attachments for high-risk mailboxes (reception, bookings, shared inboxes) where practical, or route them to review.
  • Harden MFA: prioritise app-based authentication (or security keys) over SMS where possible, especially for email and finance systems.
  • Prepare for SIM-swap: ensure mobile accounts have a port/freeze or additional security where your operator supports it; document how to regain control quickly.
  • Finance control: remind the team that any “urgent refund” or “chargeback” request must go through your normal approval steps.

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you detect and block common remote access tools unless explicitly approved (application allow-listing / controls)?
  • Do we have email attachment controls for ZIP files (especially on shared/front-desk inboxes) and safe ways to receive customer documents?
  • Are our critical accounts using phishing-resistant or app-based MFA rather than SMS? What’s the plan and timeline to reduce SMS reliance?
  • Do we have alerting for unusual sign-ins and mailbox rule changes (often used after email takeover to hide replies and invoices)?
  • What is our rapid response playbook if a user installs remote access software or suspects SIM-swap (who to call, what to isolate, what to reset first)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No specific patch theme is the main issue today. This is primarily about social engineering and account protection: tighten MFA choices, reduce risky attachments, and ensure you can quickly contain a compromised endpoint or mailbox.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “Do not call numbers from unexpected delivery/receipt/refund messages and never install remote support software from unsolicited contacts—report it to IT/management immediately.”
