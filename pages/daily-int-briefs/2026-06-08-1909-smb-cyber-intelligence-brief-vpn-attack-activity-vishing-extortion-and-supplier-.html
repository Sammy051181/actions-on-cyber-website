SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief — VPN attack activity, vishing extortion, and supplier breach ripple effects

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Monday 08 June 2026, 19:09 UK time
Today’s look-out: Ransomware entry via business VPNs + vishing/extortion + supplier/SaaS breach follow‑on scams

What to look out for today

  • Active attacks against Check Point Remote Access VPN/Mobile Access being linked to ransomware activity (Qilin) in reporting today.
  • Phone-based scams (“vishing”) and in-person social engineering being used to steal data and then extort organisations (notably professional/legal/financial services).
  • Supplier/SaaS breach ripple effects: a third-party careers platform compromise disclosed by Oxford University highlights how vendor incidents can trigger follow-on phishing and credential theft attempts.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs often rely on remote access (VPN) and outsourced IT to keep operations moving. When VPN technology is actively targeted, it can become a fast path to ransomware and business disruption. Meanwhile, vishing works because it targets busy staff (reception, finance, HR, IT admins) and can bypass email controls. Supplier incidents can create believable pretexts (“we’re your platform provider”, “we need you to reset your password”, “here’s an urgent update”).

Warning signs

  • Unusual VPN activity: logins at odd hours, unfamiliar locations, repeated failed logins, or new sessions for accounts that rarely use remote access.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts or staff reporting “push fatigue” (lots of approval requests they didn’t initiate).
  • Phone calls claiming urgency from “IT support”, “Microsoft/Google”, “your MSP”, “your bank”, or “your software supplier” asking for passwords, MFA codes, remote control, or to install “support” software.
  • Supplier-themed emails referencing real platforms your organisation uses (careers/recruitment portals, payroll, finance, document signing), especially if they push a login link or password reset.
  • Sudden data access: lots of document downloads, mailbox rule changes, or unexpected forwarding configured.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • VPN compromise can be used to gain internal access quickly, move laterally, steal data, and deploy ransomware for disruption and extortion.
  • Vishing can trick staff into handing over credentials/MFA approvals or granting remote access, enabling account takeover without “breaking in” technically.
  • Supplier incidents provide credible storylines for phishing (“security notice”, “update required”, “your account impacted”), increasing the odds that staff click and sign in to a fake page.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (5 minutes): no one should share passwords or MFA codes on the phone; verify callers via a known number or internal ticketing process.
  • Check remote access exposure: confirm who has VPN access, remove dormant accounts, and ensure strong MFA is enforced for all remote access users.
  • Review VPN and admin logs for unusual access and investigate any unexplained successful logins.
  • Supplier comms hygiene: remind teams to access SaaS platforms via bookmarks/known URLs, not email links—especially for payroll/finance/recruitment.
  • Be ready for disruption: confirm your backups are recent, restorable, and protected from tampering (ask when the last restore test was completed).

Ask your IT provider

  • Are any of our systems using Check Point Remote Access VPN/Mobile Access, and are we exposed to the actively exploited scenario described in today’s reporting?
  • Do we still have any legacy/older VPN configurations in use that increase risk, and what is the plan/timeline to modernise them?
  • What monitoring is in place for unusual VPN logins and admin account activity, and who gets alerted?
  • What is our process to verify helpdesk/support calls (including calls pretending to be the MSP)?
  • If a supplier platform we use is breached, what’s our playbook for credential resets, sign-in reviews, and staff comms?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Today’s reporting includes active exploitation concerns involving Check Point Remote Access VPN/Mobile Access (including a flaw referenced as CVE-2026-50751 in some coverage). If you use Check Point for remote access, treat this as urgent: confirm updates/mitigations are applied and review VPN access logs for suspicious activity.

One action today

Run a same-day check of VPN access logs for unusual sign-ins and confirm your VPN platform is fully up to date with enforced MFA for all remote users.
