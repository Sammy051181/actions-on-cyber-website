Today’s SME cyber lookout: Microsoft 365 phishing, fake installers, and ransomware disruption risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Thursday 09 July 2026, 16:32 UK time
Today’s look-out: Credential phishing + fake software downloads leading to account takeover and business disruption

What to look out for today

Three themes worth flagging to staff and your IT support today:

  • Microsoft 365 credential phishing is getting more convincing (phishing-as-a-service aimed at stealing M365 sign-ins, including techniques that try to bypass basic MFA).
  • Fake “popular tool” downloads (e.g., bogus installer sites) that can quietly turn a work PC into a residential proxy node used for criminal activity.
  • Ransomware disruption risk continues, with reports of newer ransomware variants focusing on disabling endpoint security before encrypting.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

For many SMEs, Microsoft 365 is the front door to email, files and invoices. If an attacker takes over one account, they can:

  • send convincing invoice/payment-change emails from a real mailbox,
  • steal data from SharePoint/OneDrive,
  • set up hidden forwarding rules to stay in your conversations, and
  • use the compromised account to access other systems via password resets.

Separately, fake software installers are a realistic risk in small teams where people “just download the tool they need” without IT review. Even if you don’t notice immediate damage, an infected machine can create compliance, reputational, and connectivity issues (and may be a stepping-stone to bigger compromise).

Warning signs

  • Unexpected Microsoft 365 login prompts, or messages saying “action required”, “urgent security update”, “quarantine release”, or “new voicemail/shared document”.
  • MFA prompts that staff did not initiate (approval pop-ups, device-code style prompts, or repeated sign-in requests).
  • Reports of emails “sent from me” that the user didn’t send, or new inbox rules/forwarding to unknown addresses.
  • Staff searching for and downloading installers from lookalike websites, or using “free download” pages instead of approved sources.
  • Endpoint protection suddenly disabled, “tamper protection” alerts, or devices becoming unusually slow/noisy on the network.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Phishing kits and ‘phishing-as-a-service’ make it easier for criminals to run targeted Microsoft 365 credential theft campaigns at scale, often tailored to your industry.
  • Account takeover can be followed by invoice fraud: attackers watch email threads and time a payment-change request to coincide with real jobs and real invoices.
  • Fake installers can add unwanted software that turns your device into part of a proxy network, giving criminals cover for other crimes and potentially opening paths for further access.
  • Ransomware operators increasingly try to neutralise defences before encrypting, aiming to increase the chance of business-wide disruption.

What to do today

  • Run a 10-minute anti-phishing reminder for all staff: never approve unexpected sign-in prompts; report them immediately.
  • Confirm your payment-change process: finance teams should require a trusted call-back (using a known number) before changing bank details.
  • Check for risky mailbox settings: review forwarding rules, delegated access, and suspicious inbox rules for key users (finance, directors, HR, payroll).
  • Lock down software installs: where possible, require admin approval for new software and provide an approved software request route.
  • Backups and restore reality check: confirm you can restore critical files and key systems (not just that backups exist).

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have alerts for new mailbox forwarding rules, suspicious inbox rules, and unusual sign-in locations for Microsoft 365?
  • Are we using phishing-resistant MFA where possible for admins and finance (and are legacy sign-in methods blocked)?
  • Can you provide a quick list of devices with local admin rights and a plan to reduce them?
  • What’s our current readiness if ransomware hits: restore times, offline/immutable backups, and a tested recovery run?
  • How do we prevent staff from installing “lookalike” software—do we have application control or an approved software catalogue?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If you self-host webmail or email services (less common for many SMEs), check with your provider whether any internet-facing mail/webmail components like Roundcube are in use and being maintained. For most SMEs on Microsoft 365 or hosted email, the practical focus today is phishing resistance, account monitoring, and tight control of software installs rather than chasing specific vulnerability details.

One action today

Send a same-day staff message: “Do not approve unexpected Microsoft sign-in/MFA prompts—report them immediately,” and remind finance that bank-detail changes require a call-back to a known number.
