Today’s SME cyber look-out: stealth phishing, “verification” scams, and breach-driven password resets

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Wednesday 08 July 2026, 15:40 UK time
Today’s look-out: Phishing and account takeover: “verification steps”, hidden phishing pages, and breach follow-on scams

What to look out for today

Be on alert for a new wave of phishing and account-takeover (ATO) attempts that are designed to bypass traditional email checks and trick staff during “verification” steps. Also expect a rise in credential-stuffing and impersonation attempts following a major telecom/ISP email platform breach.

  • “Ghost phishing” that hides the malicious page until it runs inside the victim’s browser (so basic link scanning may not spot it).
  • “Verification” lures (e.g. fake security checks, CAPTCHA/verification steps) that push users into unsafe actions.
  • Breach follow-on scams: password reset emails/SMS, “your account will be suspended” notices, and fake support calls referencing leaked credentials.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs rely heavily on Microsoft 365 (or similar cloud email), shared inboxes, and third-party IT support. If an attacker gets into one mailbox, they can:

  • Intercept invoices and change bank details (payment diversion).
  • Request payroll/HR data from finance or admin teams.
  • Use real email threads to make scams look legitimate.
  • Reset passwords for other systems where email is the recovery method.

Separately, large-scale telecom/ISP breaches can quickly translate into more convincing phishing, because attackers have real email addresses (and sometimes passwords) to test across other services.

Warning signs

  • Emails that say you must complete a “verification” or “security check” to view a document, release a message, or keep an account active.
  • Any page or email that tells staff to copy/paste something, run a command, or “open PowerShell/Terminal” as part of a verification step.
  • Login pages that look normal, but appear after an extra step (CAPTCHA/“I’m not a robot”/“decrypting content”).
  • Unexpected password reset prompts, MFA/verification codes arriving when nobody is logging in, or repeated sign-in alerts.
  • Customers/suppliers receiving odd replies from your mailbox, or your sent items showing messages you didn’t send.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Evasive phishing pages: attackers use techniques where the malicious content only becomes visible once loaded in a real browser, reducing the effectiveness of simple URL reputation checks.
  • ATO through the “verification step”: as basic credential stuffing becomes harder, attackers focus on social engineering around sign-in and account recovery flows.
  • ClickFix-style social engineering: victims are tricked into performing steps themselves under the guise of verification (for example, fake CAPTCHA/validation pages).
  • Breach ripple effects: exposed email addresses/passwords from a telecom/ISP platform breach may be tried against business email and SaaS services, or used to make targeted phishing more believable.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff warning: “No verification process will ever ask you to run commands or copy/paste steps. Report it immediately.”
  • Prioritise finance and shared inboxes: ensure MFA is enabled and monitored, and restrict auto-forwarding rules.
  • Check for mailbox rule abuse: look for new inbox rules, forwarding, or deleted security alerts.
  • Review password hygiene: if you suspect any password reuse, change it now and turn on MFA everywhere available.
  • Make payment change controls explicit: confirm bank detail changes out-of-band (known phone number, not email).

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have alerts for impossible travel, repeated login failures, and new mailbox forwarding rules in Microsoft 365 (or our email platform)?
  • Can you confirm external auto-forwarding is blocked (or tightly controlled) for all mailboxes, including shared ones?
  • What’s our standard response if a user enters credentials on a phishing page (session reset, token revocation, mailbox rule review, customer/supplier warning)?
  • Are we using phish-resistant MFA where practical (and are we actively reducing SMS-based verification where it’s high risk)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your business (or IT provider) uses AI workflow/agent-building tools such as Langflow, note that CISA has highlighted an actively exploited authentication bypass issue. SMEs should treat this as a supplier/stack question: confirm whether it exists anywhere in your environment (including dev/testing) and whether it’s been remediated.

One action today

Email all staff today: “If any website/email asks you to ‘verify’ by copying/pasting steps or running a command, stop and report it—do not proceed.”
