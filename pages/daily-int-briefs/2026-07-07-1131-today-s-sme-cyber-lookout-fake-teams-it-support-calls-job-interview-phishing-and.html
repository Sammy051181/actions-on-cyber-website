Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SME cyber lookout: fake Teams ‘IT support’ calls, job-interview phishing, and remote-access tool risk

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Tuesday 07 July 2026, 11:31 UK time
Today’s look-out: Impersonation-led access theft (Teams + Google) and remote-access supplier risk

What to look out for today

Three themes SMEs should actively brief staff on today:

  • Fake IT support calls on Microsoft Teams attempting to talk staff into installing software (malware) or granting access.
  • “Job interview” phishing impersonating well-known brands to steal Google account logins (especially targeting marketing/communications roles).
  • Remote-access supplier risk: BeyondTrust has warned of serious issues in some remote support / privileged remote access products. If you use these tools (or your IT provider does), confirm mitigations are in place.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Smaller organisations often rely on Microsoft 365/Teams, Google Workspace accounts, and outsourced IT/remote support. If an attacker gets a foothold via a convincing call or login page, it can quickly lead to mailbox compromise, invoice/payment fraud, data theft, or ransomware disruption.

Warning signs

  • A Teams voice call from “IT” you weren’t expecting, especially with urgency (“we need to fix this now”).
  • Requests to install a “support tool”, “security update”, or “remote access” app during the call.
  • Pressure to bypass normal process (no ticket number, refusal to email from an official address, or discouraging you from calling back).
  • Job-related emails/messages asking you to sign in with Google to view an interview brief, assessment, or calendar invite—especially if the link goes to a lookalike page.
  • Unexpected prompts for Google/Microsoft sign-in, MFA resets, or recovery email/phone changes.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Social engineering via Teams: attackers impersonate internal IT/support to persuade a user to install a remote-access tool or malware, giving them a way into your systems.
  • Credential theft: fake interview processes or brand impersonation pages capture Google usernames/passwords, then attackers attempt to access email, files, ads accounts, and customer data.
  • Supplier/remote-access exposure: weaknesses in remote support tooling can be attractive to attackers because one compromised remote-access path can lead to rapid access across systems.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (10 minutes): IT will never ask you to install software from a Teams call without a logged ticket and call-back verification.
  • Set a call-back rule: if “IT” calls, hang up and call the known helpdesk number (from your internal directory/contract), not a number provided in the call.
  • Harden Google accounts: confirm MFA is enabled, recovery details are correct, and staff know to report unexpected login prompts.
  • Report fast: if anyone installed anything or entered credentials, treat it as a likely compromise and escalate immediately (don’t wait for proof).

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we allow external Teams calls to reach staff? If yes, what controls are in place to reduce impersonation risk?
  • What is our verified support process (ticketing + call-back) and how is it communicated to users?
  • Do we (or you) use BeyondTrust Remote Support / Privileged Remote Access anywhere in our environment? If yes, what mitigations/updates have been applied and how do we confirm?
  • What monitoring/alerting is in place for new device sign-ins, MFA changes, and mailbox rule creation in Microsoft/Google?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your organisation (or managed service provider) uses BeyondTrust Remote Support (RS) or Privileged Remote Access (PRA), treat today as a prompt to confirm updates/mitigations have been applied and that remote-access logs are being reviewed for unusual authentication activity.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “If anyone calls you on Teams claiming to be IT and asks you to install software or share codes, hang up and call the helpdesk number from the directory—don’t continue the call.”
