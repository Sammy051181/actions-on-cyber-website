Today’s SMB Cyber Brief: Salesforce app integration disabled, MFA-bypass phishing, and ransomware defence evasion

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Friday 19 June 2026, 16:42 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier/SaaS integration risk + MFA-bypass phishing leading to account takeover

What to look out for today

  • Salesforce supplier/integration incident: Salesforce has disabled the Klue “Battlecards” app integration after OAuth token abuse linked to a security incident. If you use Salesforce and connected apps, expect disruption and potential follow-on scams.
  • Phishing that bypasses MFA (Device Code phishing): attacks that don’t need your password can still get into Microsoft/Google-style cloud accounts if staff approve the wrong sign-in flow.
  • Ransomware groups improving defence evasion: reports highlight ransomware operators using tools intended to disable endpoint security controls (EDR), increasing the chance a single foothold becomes a full business outage.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

SMEs are heavily dependent on SaaS (CRM, finance, email) and app integrations. When a supplier disables an integration, it creates both operational disruption and a window for criminals to run convincing “supplier support” scams. Separately, MFA-bypass phishing and ransomware defence evasion raise the likelihood that one successful click or approval turns into mailbox takeover, invoice fraud, data exposure, or downtime.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected alerts that an app has been disconnected from Salesforce, or users can’t access “battlecards”/related features they normally use.
  • Emails/DMs/calls claiming to be from “Salesforce”, “Klue”, or your IT provider asking you to reconnect an app, “re-authorise access”, or approve a sign-in urgently.
  • MFA prompts or sign-in approval requests that staff were not expecting (especially “enter this code on another device” / “approve sign-in”).
  • Sudden changes to mail rules, forwarding, new OAuth app consents, or users reporting “my inbox looks different”.
  • Endpoint security suddenly disabled, devices rebooting unexpectedly, or multiple PCs showing the same new “security update/tool” at once.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • OAuth/app-consent abuse: criminals try to trick a user/admin into granting a malicious app access to email/CRM data, bypassing passwords and sometimes bypassing normal MFA expectations.
  • Supplier-incident impersonation: criminals time phishing to coincide with real disruption (“integration disabled”) to pressure staff into clicking fake reconnect links or sharing credentials.
  • Account takeover → payment fraud: once inside a mailbox/CRM, attackers watch invoices and attempt bank detail swaps or intercept customer/supplier comms.
  • Ransomware with defence disruption: attackers prioritise weakening endpoint security to make encryption/data theft more likely to succeed and harder to recover from.

What to do today

  • Brief staff (2 minutes): “If you get asked to reconnect Salesforce apps or approve a sign-in you didn’t start, stop and report it.”
  • Check your Salesforce connected apps: confirm which integrations are approved, who approved them, and whether anything changed this week.
  • Lock down app consent: ensure only admins can approve new OAuth apps (or require admin approval), especially for CRM and email integrations.
  • Review key inboxes: finance@, accounts@, info@ for forwarding rules, unusual delegates, or suspicious “app access” warnings.
  • Validate payment changes out-of-band: if any supplier/customer bank detail changes arrive, confirm using a known phone number (not the one in the email).
  • Backups readiness check: confirm you can restore a file set and that backups are not dependent on a single admin account.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have a policy that restricts or requires approval for new OAuth/app consents (Microsoft 365/Google Workspace/Salesforce connected apps)?
  • Can you provide a weekly report of newly authorised apps/integrations and any changes to mail forwarding rules?
  • What monitoring is in place for Device Code phishing / unusual sign-in approvals and suspicious token activity?
  • If endpoint protection is tampered with, what’s our alerting and response path (who gets called, what is isolated first, what gets preserved)?
  • For ransomware resilience, when did we last test a restore for a typical shared drive and a key SaaS dataset?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

No specific patch push from today’s items for typical SMEs. The practical focus is on identity and integrations: reduce who can approve app access, review connected apps, and tighten sign-in approval behaviour.

One action today

Send a same-day staff note: “Do not approve unexpected sign-in prompts or ‘reconnect Salesforce app’ requests—report them to IT/management immediately.”
