Weekend SMB Cyber Brief: supply-chain poisonings and ‘fake login’ pop-ups on trusted sites

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Saturday 06 June 2026, 13:01 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier & web-injection scams leading to credential theft (plus service disruption risk)

What to look out for today

Three themes are worth briefing staff and checking with your IT provider today:

  • Software supply-chain tampering: reports of a self-spreading “Miasma” worm affecting GitHub repositories (including Microsoft-owned repos) and malicious/poisoned packages in the npm ecosystem.
  • Suspicious login prompts on legitimate websites: Toshiba and Muji warned about unexpected sign-in screens appearing on their sites, potentially harvesting credentials.
  • Targeted persistence in Microsoft 365 and web servers: reporting on threat actors maintaining access to compromised Microsoft 365 environments, and separate activity targeting Microsoft IIS servers.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • If you rely on outsourced developers, web agencies, MSPs, or use modern web tools, supply-chain issues can land in your environment without you “clicking” anything.
  • Fake sign-in prompts on trusted sites can lead to Microsoft 365/email compromise, which is a common route to invoice fraud and payroll/payment diversion.
  • If attackers get into Microsoft 365, they can quietly set up rules, forwarding, and additional access that survives password changes.
  • Disruption can be the goal too: authorities are warning about active exploitation of a SolarWinds Serv-U issue to crash servers.

Warning signs

  • Staff report being asked to sign in again on a website they don’t normally log into, or a login box appears as a pop-up/overlay that “looks a bit off”.
  • Unexpected MFA prompts, password reset emails, or sign-in alerts for Microsoft 365.
  • New/unknown email forwarding rules, auto-replies, or mailbox delegates in Microsoft 365.
  • Your developer/IT supplier flags unusual dependency changes, sudden package updates, or unexplained build failures.
  • Sudden service instability for file transfer or edge/network management tooling (potentially indicating active exploitation or attempted disruption).

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Dependency poisoning: slipping malicious code into packages or repos so it runs during builds or on developer machines, then steals secrets (API keys, tokens) that unlock cloud services.
  • “Trusted site” credential harvesting: injecting a fake login prompt into a legitimate website to capture usernames/passwords (and sometimes MFA codes).
  • Microsoft 365 takeover: using stolen credentials/tokens to access mailboxes, then targeting finance teams with convincing replies from real threads.
  • Business disruption: exploiting weaknesses in exposed services to crash systems, forcing downtime and urgent support activity.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff note (especially to finance and anyone who uses supplier portals): “If a website unexpectedly asks you to log in, stop and report it—don’t enter credentials.”
  • Check Microsoft 365 basics: review sign-in alerts and ensure MFA is enforced for all users (pay special attention to admins and finance).
  • Finance control: re-brief your payment-change process—no bank detail changes from email alone; verify via a known phone number.
  • If you have developers (internal or outsourced): ask them to pause unreviewed dependency updates this weekend and to review recent package/repo changes before the next release.
  • If you use managed file transfer / remote management tools: confirm they are monitored and internet exposure is justified.

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you show us recent Microsoft 365 sign-in anomalies and whether any accounts have risky sign-ins or impossible travel events?
  • Do we have alerts for new mailbox forwarding rules, new OAuth app consents, or new admin role assignments?
  • Which suppliers/tools we use depend on npm or pull code from public GitHub—do they have controls for dependency verification and build integrity?
  • Are any of our services (e.g., file transfer or network management consoles) exposed to the internet, and what additional monitoring is in place?
  • What is our rollback plan if a supplier update introduces malicious or unstable code?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

CISA has warned of active exploitation of a patched SolarWinds Serv-U issue to crash servers, and Cisco has warned of active exploitation affecting Catalyst SD-WAN Manager with no patch available at the time of reporting. If you use either product (directly or via an MSP), ask what mitigations/monitoring are in place and whether any internet-exposed instances exist.

One action today

Send a short internal warning: “If any website shows an unexpected login prompt or asks you to sign in again, stop and report it—don’t enter credentials.”
