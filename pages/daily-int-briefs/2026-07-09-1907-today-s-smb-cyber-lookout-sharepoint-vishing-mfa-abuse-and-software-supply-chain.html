Today’s SMB cyber lookout: SharePoint vishing + MFA abuse, and software supply‑chain traps

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Thursday 09 July 2026, 19:07 UK time
Today’s look-out: Microsoft 365/SharePoint account takeover via vishing and MFA tricks; supply‑chain and developer tool risk

What to look out for today

1) “Helpdesk / Microsoft” phone calls leading to Microsoft 365 sign-ins (vishing). A new extortion-focused group has been reported using phone-led tactics to get staff to approve sign-in prompts or follow a login flow that hands attackers access to SharePoint data.

2) Developer and supplier toolchain risk. If your business builds software (or uses a dev agency), AI coding assistants and package installs can increase the chance of accidentally pulling in the wrong dependency or triggering security tools in ways that hide real attacker activity.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • SharePoint is often the filing cabinet: contracts, HR documents, finance spreadsheets, customer lists and board papers. Data theft can quickly become extortion even without ransomware.
  • Phone-based scams bypass “don’t click links” training. Staff may comply because the caller sounds authoritative, “helpful”, or claims there’s an urgent account/security issue.
  • Outsourced IT and SaaS dependence: if an attacker gets one staff login, they can often access shared files, Teams content, and third-party apps connected to Microsoft 365.
  • Supply-chain incidents hit SMEs indirectly: a single compromised dependency or mistaken package name can affect your website, internal tools, or client deliverables.

Warning signs

  • Unexpected calls claiming to be Microsoft, your IT provider, or “security” asking you to approve an MFA prompt, read out a code, or follow steps “to fix” your account.
  • Staff report multiple MFA prompts they didn’t initiate, or a caller says “you’ll get a code/prompt—just approve it”.
  • Unusual SharePoint/OneDrive activity: lots of downloads, new sync activity, or many files accessed in a short period.
  • Developers (internal or agency) report endpoint alerts after using AI coding tools; or a sudden push to install “a quick helper package” with a plausible name.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Vishing to identity takeover: attackers use phone pressure to get a user to complete a sign-in flow or approve authentication, then pivot into SharePoint to copy data for extortion.
  • MFA abuse: repeated prompts (“MFA fatigue”) or social engineering to get a user to approve a login they didn’t start.
  • Supply-chain confusion: AI assistants (and humans) can suggest packages or tools that sound real but aren’t; attackers can register lookalike names and wait for someone to install them.

What to do today

  • Run a 10-minute staff reminder: “We will never ask you on a phone call to approve an MFA prompt or share a code. Hang up and call IT on a known number.”
  • Set a clear internal process for suspected account compromise: who to contact, what to capture (time of call, number, what was asked), and how quickly accounts are locked/reset.
  • Review SharePoint access basics: ensure sensitive libraries have least-privilege access and that ex-staff/contractor access is removed promptly.
  • If you have developers or an agency: require dependency controls (approved registries, code review for new dependencies, and a “stop and check” rule for AI-suggested package/tool names).

Ask your IT provider

  • What controls do we have to reduce MFA social-engineering risk (e.g., stronger sign-in methods) and how do we respond to repeated MFA prompts?
  • Do we have alerting for unusual SharePoint/OneDrive downloads and suspicious sign-ins, and who gets notified?
  • Can you confirm our leavers process removes Microsoft 365 access, tokens and third-party app access promptly?
  • If we use developers: what is our software supply-chain policy (approved package sources, code review requirements, and monitoring for risky dependency changes)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If your organisation runs operational/industrial or facilities equipment (e.g., power/UPS management or protection relays), check with your facilities/IT support whether any Schneider Electric components are in use and being maintained, as new vendor advisories are out. This is mainly relevant where these systems are connected to business networks.

One action today

Send a same-day message to all staff: “If anyone phones claiming to be Microsoft/IT and asks you to approve an MFA prompt or share a code, hang up and report it—do not approve anything you didn’t start.”
