Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: better phishing (login theft) and risky browser add‑ons

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Thursday 25 June 2026, 16:12 UK time
Today’s look-out: Account takeover & phishing (browser-in-the-middle) plus browser extension risk

What to look out for today

Two practical risks are standing out in reporting today:

  • More convincing Microsoft/Google-style login phishing using “browser-in-the-middle” techniques designed to capture logins (and sometimes session access) even when the page looks normal.
  • Browser extension risk: a very widely installed Chrome ad blocker for YouTube has been reported as having the capability to inject scripts (even if dormant), which is a red flag for business devices.

Why this matters to smaller businesses

Most SME compromises still start with a stolen login or a trusted device being abused. If an attacker gets into email, Microsoft 365/Google Workspace, payroll, accounting, CRM or your IT support portal, they can:

  • send convincing invoice/payment-change emails from a real account
  • steal customer data and contacts for further scams
  • reset passwords elsewhere (“password reset domino effect”)
  • use the account to access files, SharePoint/Drive, and internal chat

Meanwhile, browser add-ons are a common “silent” route to tracking, data exposure, and account theft—especially on shared office machines.

Warning signs

  • Staff report being asked to “sign in again” to email/SharePoint/OneDrive/Google Drive unexpectedly.
  • Login pages that look normal, but appear after clicking a link in an email, Teams/Chat, a PDF, or a shared document.
  • Repeated MFA prompts (“Approve sign-in?”) that staff didn’t initiate.
  • New browser extensions appearing, or extensions requesting broader permissions than expected.
  • Unusual mailbox rules, forwarding settings, or “Sent Items” messages staff don’t recognise.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Phishing-as-a-service platforms lower the barrier: attackers can quickly launch high-volume, branded login lures that capture credentials at scale.
  • Browser-in-the-middle approaches can make the victim feel like they’re signing into a real service, reducing suspicion and increasing success rates.
  • Malicious or compromised extensions can potentially read or modify what users see in the browser, inject unwanted scripts, or harvest data—turning a normal browsing session into an account risk.

What to do today

  • Send a 2-minute staff alert: don’t re-authenticate from links; instead open the service from bookmarks or by typing the known address.
  • Check your high-impact accounts: finance, payroll, admins, shared inboxes—review MFA methods, recovery emails, and recent sign-in activity.
  • Lock down browser extensions on work devices: remove “nice to have” add-ons, and only allow approved extensions (especially on finance/admin machines).
  • Review mailbox forwarding & rules: attackers often set hidden forwarding to watch invoices and payment threads.
  • Reduce exposure from shared devices: ensure separate user logins, and no shared browser profiles for finance/admin tasks.

Ask your IT provider

  • Can you restrict/approve browser extensions centrally for Chrome/Edge on company devices?
  • Do we have conditional access / sign-in risk alerts enabled for Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace?
  • How quickly do we get alerted on new inbox forwarding rules, suspicious logins, or impossible travel?
  • Which accounts are most likely to be targeted for takeover (finance, execs, admins), and what extra controls are in place?
  • Do we have a tested process to contain an account takeover within the first hour (session reset, password/MFA reset, token revocation, rule cleanup)?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

Today’s key theme is less about emergency patching and more about controlling what runs in the browser (extensions) and tightening identity protections. If your IT team uses “auto-update everything” for browsers and extensions, confirm it’s paired with an allow-list approach so risky add-ons don’t spread across the business.

One action today

Message staff today: “If you’re asked to sign in again, do not use the link—close it and sign in via your usual bookmark/known address; report unexpected MFA prompts immediately.”
