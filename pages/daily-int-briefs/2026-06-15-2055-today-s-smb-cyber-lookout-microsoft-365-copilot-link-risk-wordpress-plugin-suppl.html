Daily SMB Cyber Intelligence Brief

Today’s SMB cyber lookout: Microsoft 365 Copilot link risk, WordPress plugin supply-chain compromise, and courier-based crypto scams

What small and medium-sized businesses should look out for today.

High Monday 15 June 2026, 20:55 UK time
Today’s look-out: Supplier/SaaS risk and convincing “trusted link” phishing

What to look out for today

Three themes SMEs should brief staff on and sanity-check with IT/website suppliers today:

  • Trusted Microsoft links used as bait: reporting describes a “one-click” scenario involving Microsoft 365 Copilot Enterprise Search where a link on a real microsoft.com domain could have enabled data exposure if chained with other issues.
  • WordPress marketing plugin supply-chain compromise: OptinMonster, TrustPulse, and PushEngage were reportedly impacted via a compromise of the vendor’s CDN, which could affect websites relying on those plugins.
  • Courier-based crypto investment scams: the FBI warns fraudsters are using couriers to collect money from victims of cryptocurrency investment scams (often linked to romance/investment “pig butchering” narratives).

Why this matters to smaller businesses

  • Microsoft 365 is business-critical: if email, files, and calendars are exposed, it can lead to invoice fraud, account takeover attempts, and sensitive client leakage.
  • Website plugins are “mini suppliers”: a compromised plugin distribution channel can create business disruption, reputational damage, or lead-capture tampering without you changing anything on your side.
  • Finance teams are targeted: courier collection is a sign scams are moving from online-only to real-world cash handoffs—harder to reverse and often rushed.

Warning signs

  • Emails/Teams messages urging you to click a Microsoft link to “view a Copilot report”, “security review”, “shared file search results”, or “urgent compliance message”, especially if there’s time pressure.
  • Unexpected website behaviour after no planned changes: new pop-ups, altered subscription forms, strange redirects, or sudden drops/spikes in leads.
  • “Investment opportunity” conversations that quickly move to WhatsApp/Telegram, involve “guaranteed returns”, or escalate to requests for cash/crypto and then “a courier will collect it”.
  • Staff receiving calls claiming to be “support” asking them to approve prompts or read out codes.

How attackers may exploit the situation

  • Trusted-domain social engineering: attackers lean on the fact that a link looks legitimate (e.g. microsoft.com) to get a click past sceptical users and basic URL filters.
  • Supplier compromise ripple effects: if a plugin/CDN serving site scripts is tampered with, attackers can impact many sites at once, targeting forms, visitors, or admin users.
  • Payment extraction: crypto scams increasingly use real-world steps (couriers) to bypass bank controls, chargebacks, and family/colleague intervention.

What to do today

  • Staff message (5 minutes): “Even if a link looks like Microsoft, don’t click unexpected ‘urgent’ security/compliance/Copilot messages. Report it to IT.”
  • Microsoft 365 hygiene check: review who has access to Copilot features and what content it can search/index in your tenant; ensure MFA prompts are not being approved on request.
  • WordPress check: if you use OptinMonster, TrustPulse, or PushEngage, ask your web provider to confirm what was impacted in their environment and what validation/cleanup steps were taken (including any unusual script changes served to visitors).
  • Finance scam control: reinforce that no business will send cash to a “courier” for investments/fees/taxes. Require a second-person approval for any unusual payment request, including crypto.

Ask your IT provider

  • Do we have Microsoft 365 Copilot enabled, and what data sources can it search (SharePoint, OneDrive, Teams, email)?
  • What controls do we have to reduce damage from a single click (email security, conditional access, session controls, least-privilege access to files)?
  • Do we run any sites using OptinMonster, TrustPulse, or PushEngage, and what did we do to verify our site wasn’t affected by the CDN compromise?
  • What monitoring/alerting would tell us if our website started serving unexpected scripts or redirecting users?
  • Can you help us implement a simple payment change/callback process and logging so finance scams are easier to stop?

Patch watch - only one short paragraph, and only if relevant

If you use WordPress plugins (especially marketing/lead-capture tools), treat updates and vendor incidents as urgent operational work: ensure plugins/themes are maintained, unused plugins removed, and your website supplier can quickly confirm whether a vendor-side compromise affected what your site served to visitors.

One action today

Send a short internal alert: “Do not click unexpected Microsoft ‘Copilot/compliance/security’ links; report them. No one should ever hand cash/crypto to a courier—any payment outside normal process needs a second approval.”
